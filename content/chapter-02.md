## Introduction

Aisha Obi had been a registered nurse for exactly eleven days when the charge nurse waved her into the supply room and said, simply, "Go do the wound irrigation in 412. The order's in." Aisha had assisted with wound irrigations in clinical rotations, but she had never been validated as independently competent in the procedure at this facility — and the wound in room 412 was a complex surgical dehiscence. She stood at the threshold of a decision that had nothing to do with clinical skill and everything to do with the legal and ethical architecture of nursing practice: scope of practice, competency validation, delegation, and professional accountability.

This scenario — unremarkable in its brevity and yet freighted with consequence — illustrates why legal, ethical, and professional standards are not abstract obligations nurses memorize for examinations and then set aside. They are the operating framework within which every clinical decision is made, every order is questioned or carried out, every record is entered, and every patient is protected or failed. The nurse who understands this framework is not merely compliant; she is professionally sovereign.

Chapter 2 is organized in two broad movements. The first (Sections 2.1–2.7) addresses the *legal and regulatory* foundations of nursing practice: what the Nurse Practice Act authorizes and limits, how delegation works, what distinguishes negligence from malpractice, how HIPAA governs information, what the law requires nurses to report, where professional boundaries are drawn, and how licensure is obtained and maintained. The second movement (Sections 2.8–2.13) addresses the *ethical* foundations: the four bioethical principles, the ANA Code of Ethics, frameworks for ethical decision-making, the phenomenon of moral distress and resilience, the role of ethics committees, and nursing's enduring commitment to social justice and health equity. Together, these thirteen sections provide the legal and moral fluency every nurse needs to practice with confidence, integrity, and accountability.

## Section 2.1 — The Nurse Practice Act: State Standards and Scope of Practice

### 2.1.1 Purpose and Authority of the Nurse Practice Act

Every state and United States territory has enacted a **Nurse Practice Act (NPA)** — a body of statute law that defines what nursing is, who may practice it, under what conditions, and with what consequences for violation. The NPA is both enabling legislation, conferring the authority to practice nursing within defined parameters, and limiting legislation, establishing the outer boundary beyond which practice constitutes an unlicensed act or an unauthorized exercise of medicine.

The NPA traces its modern form to the early twentieth century. New Zealand enacted the world's first national nursing registration law in 1901; the first American state NPA was passed in North Carolina in 1903 (Catalano, 2020). By 1923, all 48 states had some form of nursing legislation. These early laws were largely permissive — they protected the title "registered nurse" but did not clearly delineate what nurses could and could not do. The shift toward more detailed scope-of-practice language accelerated after World War II as the complexity of clinical practice increased and the liability landscape expanded.

Today, each state's NPA is administered by a **state board of nursing (BON)** — a governmental agency appointed by the governor and accountable to the state legislature. The BON's authority derives directly from the NPA and typically includes the power to:

- Establish and revise standards for nursing education programs
- Administer the licensure examination and process applications
- Define the scope of practice for each level of nursing licensure (LPN/LVN, RN, APRN)
- Investigate complaints and conduct disciplinary proceedings
- Issue, suspend, or revoke nursing licenses
- Promulgate rules and regulations that carry the force of law

> **SIDEBAR — Finding Your State's NPA: NCSBN Resources**  
> The National Council of State Boards of Nursing (NCSBN) maintains a directory of all state boards of nursing at ncsbn.org. Each board's website publishes the complete text of the state NPA and all current administrative rules. Students should read their state's NPA before entering clinical practice — not merely the sections summarized in textbooks, but the full text governing RN scope of practice in their jurisdiction.

The Nursing Practice Act is distinct from but intersects with other bodies of law: federal regulations (such as Medicare Conditions of Participation), state administrative codes, and institutional policies. When these sources conflict, the hierarchy generally follows: federal law → state law (NPA) → state administrative rules → institutional policy → unit-level protocols. A policy that authorizes nurses to perform a task the NPA prohibits is legally unenforceable and exposes both the institution and the nurse to liability.

### 2.1.2 Defining Scope of Practice

**Scope of practice** describes the full range of professional activities a nurse is authorized to perform by virtue of their education, license, and demonstrated competence. It is simultaneously a legal concept (what the NPA permits), an educational concept (what the nurse was trained to do), and a competency concept (what the nurse is currently capable of doing safely).

The NPA typically differentiates scope by licensure level:

- **Licensed Practical/Vocational Nurses (LPN/LVN):** Practice under the supervision of an RN, APRN, or physician; generally limited to technical tasks with more predictable outcomes (e.g., medication administration within defined parameters, wound dressing changes, vital signs). Their scope is narrower, reflecting a shorter educational preparation (typically 12–18 months).
- **Registered Nurses (RN):** Practice independently within the nursing process; authorized to assess, diagnose (nursing diagnosis), plan, implement, and evaluate care. The RN bears the full scope of nursing accountability and may delegate to LPN/LVNs and unlicensed assistive personnel (UAPs).
- **Advanced Practice Registered Nurses (APRNs):** Nurse practitioners, clinical nurse specialists, certified registered nurse anesthetists, and certified nurse-midwives practice at an expanded scope that may include independent prescriptive authority, diagnosis of medical conditions, and ordering diagnostic tests, depending on state law.

A foundational legal standard governing nursing practice is the **reasonable, prudent nurse** standard: the level of care, skill, and treatment that a reasonably prudent nurse with similar training and experience would provide under similar circumstances. This standard is used to evaluate nursing actions in civil litigation and regulatory proceedings alike. It is not a standard of perfection — it acknowledges human error — but it establishes a floor below which practice constitutes negligence.

A critical concept that confuses many new nurses is the relationship between institutional policies and legal scope. An institution may *restrict* what a nurse does (setting narrower limits than the NPA allows) but may not *expand* what a nurse does beyond the NPA's boundaries. If a hospital policy permits RNs to perform a procedure the NPA does not authorize, the nurse who performs that procedure based on policy alone is practicing outside legal scope. The NPA governs; policy does not override law.

### 2.1.3 Consequences of Practicing Outside Scope

Practicing outside legal scope of practice exposes the nurse to consequences in three distinct systems: regulatory, civil, and criminal.

**Regulatory consequences** are imposed by the state board of nursing and may include:

- *Formal reprimand* — a documented finding that enters the nurse's permanent licensure record
- *Probation* — continued practice under conditions and with monitoring
- *Suspension* — temporary revocation of the license
- *Revocation* — permanent loss of the license to practice in that state

BON proceedings are administrative, not criminal, and operate under a lower standard of proof (preponderance of the evidence rather than beyond reasonable doubt). Findings are public in most states and may affect licensure in other states through the Nurse Licensure Compact (see Section 2.7).

**Civil consequences** arise when out-of-scope practice causes patient harm and the patient (or patient's estate) sues for damages. The elements of a successful negligence or malpractice claim are discussed in Section 2.3. Out-of-scope practice is a powerful indicator of breach of the standard of care.

**Criminal consequences** are less common but possible when out-of-scope practice is sufficiently egregious — for example, a nurse performing a procedure with a fatal outcome that is unambiguously outside any reasonable interpretation of nursing scope may face charges of criminally negligent homicide or practicing medicine without a license.

Documentation is the nurse's most important legal protection. A contemporaneous, accurate, objective clinical record demonstrates that the nurse acted within scope, followed protocols, communicated relevant findings, and responded appropriately to changes in patient condition. The maxim "if it isn't charted, it wasn't done" has real legal weight: courts and boards routinely treat the absence of documentation as evidence that an action was not taken.

## Section 2.2 — Delegation: The Five Rights of Delegation

### 2.2.1 Delegation Defined

**Delegation** is the transfer of responsibility for the performance of a nursing task from a licensed nurse to another individual, while the delegating nurse retains accountability for the outcome. This definition contains three essential elements that are frequently misunderstood.

First, *responsibility* for the task transfers — the person performing the task is responsible for doing it correctly. Second, *accountability* does not fully transfer — the delegating RN remains accountable for the appropriateness of the delegation decision, for providing adequate direction, and for supervising the outcome. Third, delegation requires a *competent recipient* — the individual receiving the delegated task must have demonstrated the knowledge and skill to perform it safely.

Delegation must be distinguished from two related but distinct concepts:

- **Assignment** refers to the routine distribution of work that is within the job description and scope of the person receiving it. When a charge nurse assigns a patient to a staff nurse, or a staff nurse assigns a vital signs check to a UAP per established protocol, that is assignment, not delegation.
- **Supervision** is the active process of overseeing another's work, providing direction, monitoring performance, and evaluating outcomes. The delegating nurse has a duty to supervise whenever delegation occurs.

The governing framework for nursing delegation in the United States is the **NCSBN National Guidelines for Nursing Delegation** (National Council of State Boards of Nursing [NCSBN], 2016). These guidelines apply to RN and LPN/LVN delegation to other nurses and to UAPs and provide the foundation for the Five Rights of Delegation.

### 2.2.2 The Five Rights of Delegation

The NCSBN and the American Nurses Association (ANA) jointly published a position statement endorsing the Five Rights of Delegation as the practical framework for safe delegation decisions (ANA & NCSBN, 2019). Each right represents a question the delegating nurse must answer affirmatively before proceeding.

**1. Right Task**  
Is this task appropriate for delegation? A task is appropriate for delegation when it meets all of the following criteria: (a) it is within the delegatee's job description and training; (b) it does not require independent nursing assessment, judgment, or the nursing process; (c) it has a predictable outcome in a stable patient; and (d) it is permitted by the state NPA and institutional policy. Tasks that are *never* appropriate for delegation include nursing assessment, nursing diagnosis, care planning, and evaluation — the core cognitive activities of nursing practice.

**2. Right Circumstance**  
Is the patient situation appropriate for this delegation? Even a task that is generally delegable becomes inappropriate when patient acuity is elevated or the clinical situation is unstable or unpredictable. The nurse must assess whether the patient's current condition, the care setting, and the available resources support safe delegation at this moment.

**3. Right Person**  
Is the delegatee the right person for this task? The delegating nurse must verify that the delegatee has the documented competency to perform the specific task being delegated. Verbal assurance ("I've done this before") is insufficient — the RN should confirm that competency has been formally validated per institutional process.

**4. Right Direction and Communication**  
Has the nurse provided clear, complete instructions? Direction must include the specific task to be performed, the patient's relevant current status, the expected outcome, any parameters for reporting (e.g., "notify me if the blood pressure is above 160 systolic"), and the time frame. Instructions must be given directly to the delegatee — delegation cannot be relayed through a third party.

**5. Right Supervision and Evaluation**  
Has the nurse established a plan for follow-up? The delegating nurse must monitor task completion, evaluate patient outcomes, and provide feedback to the delegatee. Supervision may be direct (present during the task) or indirect (available and monitoring remotely), depending on the task complexity and delegatee experience. The nurse must be available to respond to questions or problems.

> **SAFETY ALERT — Common Delegation Errors and Their Consequences**  
> The most frequent delegation errors include: (1) delegating assessment tasks (e.g., asking a UAP to "check on" a deteriorating patient rather than conducting a nursing assessment); (2) failing to verify competency before delegating; (3) providing incomplete instructions and assuming the delegatee knows what to do; and (4) failing to follow up. Each of these errors shifts the nurse toward malpractice liability. When adverse events are investigated, documentation of the delegation decision — or the absence of such documentation — is a primary focus of review.

### 2.2.3 What Cannot Be Delegated

The nursing process in its entirety — assessment, nursing diagnosis, outcome identification and planning, implementation of nursing judgment–intensive interventions, and evaluation — belongs to the registered nurse and cannot be delegated. This is not a policy preference; it reflects the legal definition of nursing practice under virtually every state NPA.

Specific examples of non-delegable nursing activities include:

- Initial and ongoing patient assessments
- Interpretation of assessment data to form a nursing diagnosis
- Development of the nursing care plan
- Patient and family education planning and implementation
- Medication administration involving clinical judgment (e.g., titration of vasoactive infusions, nursing judgment regarding prn administration based on assessment)
- Evaluation of the patient's response to treatment
- Any task that requires the delegatee to exercise independent clinical judgment beyond their training and scope

LPN/LVNs occupy a middle position in the delegation hierarchy: they may perform many tasks under RN supervision (including many medication administrations in stable patients), but they may not perform activities that require the full scope of RN professional judgment, and they in turn may delegate limited tasks to UAPs within the boundaries their state NPA defines.

### 2.2.4 Delegation Across Care Settings

The delegation landscape varies substantially across care settings, and nurses must be alert to these differences.

In **acute care**, a formal institutional competency system typically documents which tasks UAPs and LPN/LVNs are validated to perform. The nurse can reference this system when making delegation decisions. Patient acuity is often higher and situations less predictable, requiring more conservative delegation judgment.

In **long-term care**, LPN/LVNs frequently function as the "charge nurse" for a unit, making delegation decisions to CNAs (certified nursing assistants) with RN oversight available but not always on-site. The RN in this setting has a supervisory role and must establish clear communication channels and protocols.

In **home health**, the delegation calculus is more complex: the UAP may be in the patient's home without immediate nursing oversight, and the patient and family themselves become participants in the care delivery environment. The nurse must provide extraordinarily clear written and verbal instructions and establish reliable check-in protocols.

The **Nurse Licensure Compact (NLC)** (discussed in Section 2.7) adds an additional layer of complexity: nurses practicing under a compact license in a state other than their home state must comply with the delegation rules of the *practice* state, not their home state — a distinction that can be overlooked by travel and agency nurses.

## Section 2.3 — Negligence, Malpractice, and Professional Liability

### 2.3.1 Elements of Negligence

**Negligence** is a civil wrong (tort) that occurs when a person fails to exercise the degree of care that a reasonably prudent person would exercise under the same circumstances, and that failure causes harm to another. For negligence to be legally actionable, four elements must all be present and provable by a preponderance of the evidence (more likely than not):

1. **Duty** — The nurse owed a duty of care to the patient. In nursing, duty is established by the nurse-patient relationship — specifically, by the act of accepting an assignment or initiating care. Once a nurse accepts responsibility for a patient, duty exists.

2. **Breach of duty** — The nurse failed to meet the standard of care. The standard of care is what a reasonably prudent nurse with similar education and experience would do under similar circumstances. Breach is typically established through expert nurse testimony and by reference to nursing standards, institutional policies, and professional guidelines.

3. **Causation** — The breach was the proximate cause of the harm. Causation has two components: *cause in fact* (the harm would not have occurred but for the breach) and *proximate cause* (the harm was a foreseeable consequence of the breach). A nurse who makes an error that does not cause harm has not committed actionable negligence, even if the error itself was a breach.

4. **Damages** — The patient suffered actual, quantifiable harm. Damages may include physical injury, psychological harm, additional medical costs, lost wages, and pain and suffering.

Consider a clinical example: A nurse fails to reassess a patient's IV site every two hours per protocol (duty and breach), the IV infiltrates and the vesicant medication causes tissue necrosis (causation), and the patient requires surgical debridement (damages). All four elements are present; the case is legally actionable.

### 2.3.2 Malpractice: Negligence by a Professional

**Malpractice** is negligence committed by a licensed professional — in this context, a nurse — in the course of providing professional services. The distinction from ordinary negligence is that the standard of care against which the professional's conduct is measured is a *professional* standard, not the general "reasonable person" standard. This is a higher and more demanding benchmark: the reasonably prudent nurse, not merely the reasonably prudent layperson.

Common nursing malpractice scenarios include (Brous, 2019):

- **Medication errors:** Wrong drug, wrong dose, wrong route, wrong time, wrong patient; failure to perform the rights of medication administration; failure to monitor for adverse effects.
- **Assessment failures:** Failure to identify a deteriorating patient, failure to recognize early signs of sepsis or respiratory compromise, failure to assess pain adequately.
- **Communication failures:** Failure to communicate critical values to the provider, failure to escalate when an order is inappropriate, failure to document a significant change in condition.
- **Falls:** Failure to complete a fall risk assessment, failure to implement ordered fall prevention interventions, inadequate monitoring of a high-risk patient.
- **Medication reconciliation errors:** Failure to identify and reconcile pre-admission medications at admission, transfer, and discharge.

Expert nurse testimony plays a central role in malpractice litigation. Because jurors and judges are not nursing professionals, they rely on expert witnesses to explain the applicable standard of care and whether the defendant nurse met or breached that standard. The credibility and clarity of the expert's testimony often determines the outcome of a case.

> **EBP CONNECTION — Joint Commission Sentinel Event Data and Nursing Implications**  
> The Joint Commission defines a sentinel event as an unexpected occurrence involving death or serious physical or psychological injury, or the risk thereof. The Joint Commission's annual Sentinel Event Data summaries consistently identify wrong-site surgery, falls, and medication errors among the most frequent high-severity events. Root-cause analyses of these events regularly implicate communication failures and inadequate assessment — precisely the areas where nursing practice directly affects outcomes. Nurses who stay current with sentinel event trends are better positioned to anticipate and prevent the conditions that generate malpractice claims.

### 2.3.3 Preventing Liability: Safe Practice Strategies

The most effective malpractice prevention strategy is not legal awareness but clinical excellence: consistent adherence to evidence-based standards, thorough assessment, clear communication, and meticulous documentation. The following strategies operationalize this principle.

**Know and stay within your scope.** As discussed in Section 2.1, scope violations are a reliable predictor of adverse outcomes. When uncertain about whether a task is within scope, the nurse should consult the NPA, the institutional policy, and if necessary the charge nurse, educator, or CNO before proceeding.

**Question inappropriate orders.** The nurse has both an ethical obligation and a legal one to refuse orders that would cause patient harm. The "just following orders" defense has no standing in nursing law or ethics. Nurses who carry out clearly harmful orders share liability for the resulting harm.

**Document contemporaneously and objectively.** Documentation should be completed as close in time to the event as possible (contemporaneously), describe what was observed rather than what was interpreted (objectively), and use precise clinical language. Late entries must be clearly identified as such. Corrections must be made by a single line through the error, initialed, dated, and corrected — never by obscuring original content.

**Complete incident reports.** Incident reports (variance reports, event reports) are internal quality improvement documents and are generally protected from discovery in litigation in most jurisdictions, though this protection varies. Their purpose is to capture events that deviate from expected outcomes so the institution can identify patterns and improve systems. Completing an incident report does not constitute an admission of liability; failing to complete one when required may.

**Participate in root-cause analysis.** When serious events occur, the institution's risk management and quality team will typically conduct a root-cause analysis (RCA) to identify systemic factors that contributed to the event. Nurses who participate honestly and constructively contribute to system improvement and demonstrate professional accountability.

### 2.3.4 Professional Liability Insurance

Most nurses are covered by their employer's liability insurance for acts performed within the scope of employment. However, this coverage has important limitations that every nurse should understand.

Employer coverage protects the employer's interests, not the individual nurse's. The employer's insurer will provide a defense attorney, but that attorney's primary obligation is to the employer. If the nurse's interests and the employer's interests diverge during litigation — for example, if the employer argues the nurse acted outside policy — the nurse without personal coverage has no independently retained counsel.

Individual professional liability insurance policies are available to nurses through organizations such as NSO (Nurses Service Organization) and CNA Financial Corporation. These policies provide a defense attorney who represents the nurse alone, and coverage may extend to regulatory proceedings (e.g., board of nursing investigations) that employer coverage typically does not address.

The debate over whether nurses should carry individual coverage continues (Brous, 2019). Arguments in favor include independent legal representation, coverage for off-duty advice, and board of nursing defense. Arguments against include the theoretical concern that individual coverage makes the nurse a more attractive defendant for plaintiff attorneys. Most nursing professional organizations recommend that nurses carry individual coverage.

## Section 2.4 — HIPAA, Confidentiality, and Patient Privacy in the EHR Era

### 2.4.1 HIPAA Foundations

The **Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)** of 1996 (Public Law 104-191) is the primary federal law governing the privacy and security of patient health information in the United States. While HIPAA has multiple components addressing insurance portability and administrative simplification, the provisions most directly relevant to nursing practice are the **Privacy Rule** (45 CFR Part 164, Subpart E) and the **Security Rule** (45 CFR Part 164, Subpart C), both of which took effect in 2003.

**The Privacy Rule** establishes national standards for the protection of **protected health information (PHI)** — any individually identifiable information about a person's health status, healthcare, or payment for healthcare that is created, used, or disclosed by a HIPAA-covered entity. PHI includes not only clinical information (diagnoses, medications, test results, treatment plans) but also any information that could be used to identify the patient, including name, address, date of birth, Social Security number, telephone number, and photographs.

Covered entities subject to HIPAA include healthcare providers that transmit health information electronically (virtually all hospitals, clinics, and practices), health plans, and healthcare clearinghouses. Business associates — entities that perform services for covered entities involving PHI — are also subject to HIPAA through business associate agreements.

**The Security Rule** addresses **electronic PHI (ePHI)** specifically, requiring covered entities to implement administrative, physical, and technical safeguards to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of ePHI. For nurses, the Security Rule's practical implications include unique user logins for EHR access, automatic logoff when workstations are idle, prohibition on sharing login credentials, and encryption of devices that store ePHI.

**The Breach Notification Rule** (added by the HITECH Act in 2009) requires covered entities to notify affected individuals, the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS), and in certain cases the media, when unsecured PHI is breached (impermissibly used or disclosed). Penalties for HIPAA violations range from $100 to $50,000 per violation, with a maximum of $1.9 million per violation category per year, depending on culpability. Criminal penalties, including imprisonment, may apply in cases of deliberate misuse.

### 2.4.2 Permissible Disclosures

Not all disclosures of PHI are prohibited. HIPAA explicitly permits disclosure for certain purposes without patient authorization.

**Treatment, Payment, and Healthcare Operations (TPO):** Disclosure for the purpose of treating the patient (sharing records with a consulting specialist), processing payment (billing the insurance company), or conducting healthcare operations (quality improvement activities) is permitted without authorization. This is the broadest and most frequently used exception and underlies the daily exchange of clinical information among members of the care team.

**Public Health Activities:** HIPAA permits disclosure to public health authorities for activities such as reporting communicable diseases, tracking adverse drug events, and monitoring workplace injuries. State mandatory reporting laws (see Section 2.5) typically fall within this exception.

**Law Enforcement:** Disclosure to law enforcement is permitted in limited circumstances, including compliance with a court order or subpoena, reporting a crime committed on the covered entity's premises, or assisting in the identification of a fugitive, suspect, or missing person.

**Abuse, Neglect, and Domestic Violence:** Disclosure is permitted to government authorities authorized by law to receive reports of abuse or neglect of children, the elderly, or adults with disabilities.

**The Minimum Necessary Standard:** Even when disclosure is permissible, HIPAA requires that only the minimum necessary information be disclosed to accomplish the purpose. A nurse who verbally reviews a patient's full social history with a billing specialist when only the diagnosis and procedure codes are needed would violate the minimum necessary standard.

> **CLINICAL TIP — The "Minimum Necessary" Rule in Daily EHR Use**  
> The minimum necessary standard is not just a HIPAA compliance concept — it is a daily clinical practice. Before accessing a patient's record, ask: Do I have a treatment relationship with this patient right now? Do I need this specific information to provide care? Accessing records of former patients, friends, family members, celebrities, or colleagues without a current care relationship constitutes a HIPAA violation regardless of intent. Most EHR systems generate audit logs that track every access; impermissible accesses are regularly identified through routine audits and are among the most common causes of healthcare worker termination.

### 2.4.3 Patient Rights Under HIPAA

HIPAA grants patients specific rights with respect to their own PHI:

- **Right of access:** Patients may request a copy of their own health records, including the medical record and billing records. Covered entities must provide access within 30 days (extendable once by 30 days) and may charge a reasonable, cost-based fee for copying.
- **Right to amend:** Patients may request amendment of their PHI if they believe it is incorrect or incomplete. The covered entity may deny the request if the information is accurate and complete, was not created by the entity, is not part of the designated record set, or would not be available for inspection.
- **Right to request restrictions:** Patients may request that the covered entity restrict certain uses or disclosures of their PHI. The entity is not required to agree to the restriction unless the restriction applies to disclosure to a health plan for payment or operations purposes and the care was paid out of pocket in full.
- **Right to confidential communications:** Patients may request that communications about PHI be made through an alternative means or location (e.g., "call me only on my cell phone, not my home number").
- **Right to an accounting of disclosures:** Patients may request a list of disclosures of their PHI made outside of TPO purposes during the previous six years.

### 2.4.4 EHR-Specific Privacy Challenges

The electronic health record (EHR) has transformed patient care documentation, enabling rapid access to comprehensive clinical information across care settings. It has also introduced new privacy vulnerabilities that nurses must understand and actively guard against.

**Audit trails and inappropriate access** are among the most common HIPAA enforcement issues in healthcare organizations. EHRs generate a detailed access log for every record: who accessed it, when, from which device, and which elements were viewed or modified. Healthcare organizations routinely audit these logs and investigate anomalous access patterns — including access to records of VIP patients, employees, family members, and individuals involved in high-profile events. Nurses have been terminated and licensed disciplined for accessing records of neighbors, celebrities, and even their own children (when not actively involved in care) without a legitimate clinical need.

**Copy-paste errors** are a documentation quality and safety concern specific to EHRs. The ability to copy previous nursing notes and paste them into current documentation creates the risk of outdated, inaccurate, or misleading information propagating forward in the record. Several high-profile adverse events have been linked to clinical decisions made on the basis of erroneous copy-paste content. Nurses should document each assessment from current observation, not from copied prior notes.

**Social media and incidental identification** represent a contemporary threat to patient privacy that operates at the boundary of HIPAA and professional conduct. A photograph that does not show the patient's face but does show a distinctive tattoo, a room number, a window view, or a family member in the background may be sufficient to identify the patient. Even descriptions of a "young woman admitted for a gunshot wound on Christmas Eve" may uniquely identify an individual in a small community. The NCSBN *A Nurse's Guide to the Use of Social Media* (2018) explicitly addresses these risks.
