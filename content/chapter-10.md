## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

1. Compare and contrast biomedical, biopsychosocial, and holistic models of health.
2. Explain the health-illness continuum and its application to nursing assessment and planning.
3. Identify the major categories of health determinants — biological, behavioral, and social — and describe their interaction.
4. Differentiate among primary, secondary, and tertiary levels of disease prevention and provide clinical examples of each.
5. Compare the nursing implications of acute illness with those of chronic illness management.
6. Describe the sick role concept and explain how illness behavior influences patient engagement with the healthcare system.
7. Define health disparities and social determinants of health, and discuss nursing strategies to reduce health inequity.
8. Apply knowledge of wellness and illness concepts to clinical scenarios involving diverse patient populations.

## Key Terms

| Term | Brief Definition |
|------|-----------------|
| **Health** | A dynamic state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, not merely the absence of disease (WHO, 1948) |
| **Wellness** | An active, intentional process of developing awareness and making choices toward a more successful existence |
| **Illness** | A subjective experience of symptoms and suffering; how a person perceives, experiences, and responds to disease |
| **Disease** | An objective, measurable alteration in body structure or function identified through clinical signs |
| **Biomedical model** | A framework that defines health as the absence of pathology and focuses on biological causes and treatments |
| **Biopsychosocial model** | An expanded framework recognizing biological, psychological, and social factors as interactive contributors to health |
| **Holistic model** | A perspective that views the whole person — body, mind, spirit, and environment — as integral to health |
| **Health-illness continuum** | A dynamic, graduated spectrum ranging from optimal wellness at one pole to severe illness or death at the other |
| **High-level wellness** | Dunn's (1961) concept of functioning at full potential within one's environment |
| **Determinants of health** | The range of personal, social, economic, and environmental factors that influence health status |
| **Health promotion** | Actions taken to increase well-being and maximize health potential, independent of disease risk |
| **Disease prevention** | Interventions designed to reduce the incidence, prevalence, or consequences of a specific disease |
| **Primary prevention** | Interventions that prevent disease before it occurs (e.g., immunization, health education) |
| **Secondary prevention** | Early detection and prompt treatment to limit disease severity (e.g., screening programs) |
| **Tertiary prevention** | Rehabilitation and management to minimize disability and restore function after disease is established |
| **Acute illness** | A health disruption of rapid onset, short duration, and typically full recovery potential |
| **Chronic illness** | A long-lasting condition requiring ongoing management, often with irreversible pathological changes |
| **Sick role** | Parsons's (1951) sociological concept defining rights and obligations of a person recognized as ill |
| **Illness behavior** | The manner in which individuals monitor their bodies, define symptoms, and seek or avoid care |
| **Health disparities** | Preventable differences in the burden of disease, injury, or violence among population groups |
| **Social determinants of health (SDOH)** | Conditions in the environments where people live, learn, work, and play that affect health outcomes |

## Introduction

Maria is a 54-year-old Latina woman who presents to a community health clinic reporting fatigue, unintended weight gain, and "not feeling like herself" for the past year. Her fasting glucose is 108 mg/dL — in the prediabetes range — and her blood pressure is 138/88 mmHg. She works two part-time jobs, lives in a food desert, has no health insurance, and has not seen a provider in six years. By every biomedical criterion, Maria is "not yet sick." She has no formal diagnosis, no acute complaint, and no immediate clinical emergency. Yet by any meaningful measure of health, Maria is not well.

This vignette illustrates why nurses must move beyond the binary question of "sick or not sick" to engage with health as a complex, continuous, and contextually embedded phenomenon. The way a nurse conceptualizes health determines how she assesses patients, designs care plans, delivers education, and advocates for populations. A nurse who defines health solely as the absence of pathology will respond to Maria with a glucose diary and a prescription pad. A nurse who grasps the full architecture of health — its social roots, its determinants, its behavioral dimensions, and its disparities — will also ask about Maria's food access, financial barriers, cultural beliefs about illness, and readiness for change.

This chapter builds that foundational architecture. It begins by examining how health and wellness have been defined across time and across theoretical frameworks (Section 10.1). It then explores the health-illness continuum as a dynamic model of where patients exist at any moment along the spectrum of well-being (Section 10.2). The chapter turns next to the determinants that drive a person's position on that continuum: biological endowment, behavioral choices, and the social and physical environment (Section 10.3). Building on that foundation, it examines health promotion and disease prevention at three levels of clinical intervention (Section 10.4), followed by the distinct nursing implications of acute and chronic illness (Section 10.5). The final two sections address illness from the patient's subjective and social perspective — the sick role and illness behavior (Section 10.6) — and the structural inequities that make health outcomes profoundly unequal across population groups (Section 10.7).

## Section 10.1 — Definitions and Models of Health and Wellness

### 10.1.1 The Evolution of Health Definitions

For most of human history, health was defined negatively — as the absence of disease, pain, or infirmity. This negative definition persisted well into the twentieth century because it was operationally convenient: a physician could declare a patient "healthy" once pathology was excluded. The limitation of this view became increasingly apparent as chronic conditions, mental health disorders, and social suffering failed to fit neatly into the disease-or-not dichotomy.

The first major paradigm shift came in 1948 when the World Health Organization (WHO) offered its foundational definition: *"Health is a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity"* (WHO, 1948, preamble). This definition was groundbreaking because it was positive — it described what health *is* rather than what it lacks — and because it incorporated mental and social dimensions alongside the physical.

Critics noted, however, that the WHO definition set an unrealistically high bar: "complete well-being" is a state most people never achieve, making almost everyone technically "unhealthy." This criticism prompted further theoretical development over subsequent decades.

In 1986, the WHO Ottawa Charter for Health Promotion refined the concept, describing health not as an end state but as *"a resource for everyday life"* — something people use to live fully, not a condition to be achieved and maintained (WHO, 1986). This resource framing has important implications for nursing: it positions the nurse as someone who *builds* patient capacity, not merely treats deficits.

### 10.1.2 Theoretical Models of Health

Four major conceptual models have shaped nursing's understanding of health:

**The Biomedical Model**

The biomedical model, dominant in Western medicine since the nineteenth century, equates health with the absence of detectable pathology. Health is achieved when homeostatic mechanisms function within normal ranges; disease is a deviation from biological norms caused by pathogens, genetic defects, or physiological dysfunction (Engel, 1977). The biomedical model excels in acute care settings where a discrete cause-and-effect chain links pathogen to pathology to treatment. It falls short, however, when dealing with conditions whose etiologies are multifactorial, psychosocial, or embedded in systemic inequity.

**The Biopsychosocial Model**

Psychiatrist George Engel (1977) proposed the biopsychosocial model as a direct critique of the biomedical framework's reductionism. Engel argued that illness arises from the interaction of biological vulnerabilities (genetics, physiology), psychological factors (thoughts, emotions, coping styles), and social context (relationships, culture, socioeconomic status). No single factor is sufficient to explain any given health state. The biopsychosocial model is now foundational to nursing education and is explicitly reflected in the Nursing Process, which mandates assessment of psychological and social domains alongside physical findings.

**The Holistic Model**

The holistic model extends biopsychosocial thinking by incorporating spiritual, environmental, and transcultural dimensions. Originating in humanistic nursing theory — particularly in the work of Jean Watson (1979) and the unitary human being perspective of Martha Rogers (1970) — holism posits that the person cannot be understood by analyzing parts in isolation. The whole is greater than the sum of its components, and healing requires attending to the full person-environment system. The American Holistic Nurses Association (AHNA) operationalizes this in practice standards that encompass mind-body-spirit interventions, self-care by the nurse, and healing environments (AHNA, 2019).

**The Wellness Model**

Halbert Dunn, often called the father of the modern wellness movement, introduced the concept of *high-level wellness* in 1961, defining it as *"an integrated method of functioning which is oriented toward maximizing the potential of which the individual is capable"* (Dunn, 1961, p. 4). Wellness, in Dunn's framework, is not a destination but a direction — an active, intentional orientation toward growth, balance, and fulfillment. This model is particularly important in health promotion nursing, where the goal is not merely to restore a patient to a pre-illness baseline but to support the patient in moving toward a richer expression of human potential.

### 10.1.3 Dimensions of Wellness

Most contemporary wellness frameworks identify six to eight interconnected dimensions:

| Dimension | Description | Nursing Relevance |
|-----------|-------------|------------------|
| **Physical** | Fitness, nutrition, sleep, absence of disease | Foundational assessment domain |
| **Emotional** | Self-awareness, coping, resilience, emotional regulation | Mental health screening, therapeutic communication |
| **Social** | Relationships, community, sense of belonging | Social history, isolation risk assessment |
| **Intellectual** | Curiosity, learning, problem-solving | Health literacy assessment, patient education |
| **Spiritual** | Purpose, meaning, values, transcendence | Spiritual assessment (FICA, HOPE tools) |
| **Environmental** | Safe, supportive physical surroundings | Home safety, community health assessment |
| **Occupational** | Meaningful work and role function | Functional status, return-to-work planning |
| **Financial** | Perceived economic security; ability to meet basic needs | SDOH screening, referral to social work |

No dimension operates in isolation. Research consistently demonstrates that disruption in one domain cascades into others: chronic financial stress (financial wellness) activates neuroendocrine pathways that impair immune function (physical wellness) and erode sleep quality, which in turn undermines emotional regulation (emotional wellness) and capacity for social engagement (social wellness) (McEwen, 2012).

> **Callout — Clinical Application: Conducting a Wellness Assessment**
> Rather than restricting intake assessments to physical review-of-systems, many institutions now incorporate wellness screening tools that address all eight dimensions. The Wellness Recovery Action Plan (WRAP) and the Lifestyle Medicine assessment battery are examples. Nurses who screen across dimensions identify unmet needs — social isolation, spiritual distress, environmental hazards — that drive readmission rates and impair adherence.

## Section 10.2 — The Health-Illness Continuum

### 10.2.1 Origins and Architecture of the Continuum Model

The concept of a health-illness continuum emerged from dissatisfaction with dichotomous thinking about health. The binary sick/not-sick framework cannot represent the clinical reality of patients who are functionally impaired but not diagnosably ill, or patients with serious diagnoses who maintain high quality of life through effective self-management.

Halbert Dunn's wellness grid (1961) was among the first formal attempts to represent health as a spectrum. Dunn mapped health on two axes: *health status* (from peak wellness to death) and *environment* (from favorable to unfavorable), producing four quadrants that capture how environmental context modulates the expression of health potential. A person with excellent biological health living in a resource-poor environment occupies a fundamentally different position from someone with the same biological endowment in a resource-rich environment.

The simplified continuum model most commonly taught in nursing education arranges health states along a single horizontal axis, with *optimal wellness* at one end and *severe illness/death* at the other (Figure 10.2). This linear model, though a simplification, is clinically useful because:

1. It emphasizes that health is dynamic — individuals move along the continuum in response to illness, intervention, coping, aging, and behavior change.
2. It creates a shared conceptual language for interdisciplinary teams discussing a patient's trajectory.
3. It directs nursing assessment toward understanding not just where a patient *is* on the continuum, but which direction they are *moving* and at what rate.

### 10.2.2 The Neutral Zone

A critical clinical concept within the continuum model is the *neutral zone* — the area in the middle of the spectrum where a person is neither acutely ill nor functioning at peak wellness. The neutral zone is not a state of health; it is a state of reduced capacity, risk accumulation, and vulnerability. Many of the patients nurses encounter in ambulatory, primary care, and home health settings reside in the neutral zone.

The neutral zone is clinically important for several reasons:

- **Prevention opportunity:** Patients in the neutral zone are accessible before disease becomes irreversible. Early intervention during this window yields the greatest return on investment in terms of disease prevention and quality of life preservation.
- **Risk visibility:** Patients in the neutral zone often have multiple sub-clinical risk factors — elevated BMI, sedentary lifestyle, borderline hypertension — that individually fall below clinical thresholds but collectively represent substantial cardiovascular risk.
- **Patient perception gap:** Patients in the neutral zone frequently do not perceive themselves as ill, creating engagement challenges. The nurse who understands the neutral zone can frame health education not as "treating your disease" but as "protecting what you have and building toward more."

### 10.2.3 Dynamic Movement Along the Continuum

Health is not a stable position but a dynamic trajectory. Several forces act simultaneously on a person's position along the continuum:

**Factors that move the trajectory toward illness:**
- Pathological processes (infection, inflammation, neoplasm)
- Health-risk behaviors (tobacco use, physical inactivity, unhealthy diet)
- Adverse social determinants (poverty, housing instability, food insecurity)
- Psychosocial stressors (bereavement, trauma, caregiver burden)
- Inadequate access to preventive or curative healthcare

**Factors that move the trajectory toward wellness:**
- Effective medical and nursing treatment
- Health-promoting behaviors (exercise, balanced nutrition, adequate sleep)
- Strong social support networks
- Protective SDOH (economic stability, educational attainment, safe neighborhoods)
- Patient activation and health literacy
- Spiritual and psychological resilience

Nurses function at the intersection of these forces. Every nursing intervention — medication administration, patient education, discharge planning, care coordination — is intended to shift a patient's trajectory toward the wellness pole. Understanding the continuum model clarifies the *direction* and *purpose* of nursing care, not just its task components.

### 10.2.4 Illness vs. Disease: A Critical Distinction

The health-illness continuum highlights a distinction that biomedical thinking often conflates: the difference between *disease* and *illness*.

**Disease** is an objective, clinically verifiable alteration in body structure or function. Disease is diagnosed, classified, and treated. It is what a provider measures with instruments, laboratory values, and imaging.

**Illness** is the subjective experience of disease — how the patient *feels*, *interprets*, and *responds* to the disruption in their body and life. Two patients with identical diagnoses of type 2 diabetes may have profoundly different illness experiences based on their cultural backgrounds, prior healthcare encounters, social supports, self-efficacy beliefs, and personal meanings attributed to the diagnosis.

This distinction has direct implications for nursing assessment. A complete nursing assessment does not stop at disease identification (What is the diagnosis? What are the labs?) but extends to illness understanding (How is this patient experiencing this diagnosis? What does it mean to them? How is it affecting their daily life and relationships?). Nurses who attend only to disease miss the illness experience — and miss the opportunity to provide truly patient-centered care (Kleinman, 1988).

> **Callout — Evidence-Based Practice: Explanatory Models in Patient Assessment**
> Arthur Kleinman's Explanatory Model Interview (1988) provides a structured approach to eliciting the patient's illness experience. Eight questions explore the patient's understanding of cause, timeline, severity, feared effects, and desired treatment. Integration of Kleinman's model into nursing assessment has been associated with improved patient satisfaction and adherence in culturally diverse populations (Betancourt et al., 2016).
