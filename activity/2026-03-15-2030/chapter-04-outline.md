## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

1. Describe the components of the communication process and explain how each element influences healthcare interactions.
2. Distinguish between verbal and nonverbal communication and apply strategies to ensure message congruence in clinical settings.
3. Identify and apply the three phases of the therapeutic nurse-patient relationship (orientation, working, and termination).
4. Demonstrate therapeutic communication techniques (e.g., active listening, open-ended questions, reflection, silence) in clinical scenarios.
5. Recognize and avoid non-therapeutic communication patterns that impede trust and information exchange.
6. Apply motivational interviewing principles using the OARS technique to support behavior change in patients.
7. Adapt communication strategies across the lifespan and in high-stress or crisis situations.
8. Use SBAR and I-PASS frameworks to communicate critical patient information accurately to interdisciplinary team members.
9. Apply documentation standards, including legal considerations, to electronic health record (EHR) entries.

## Key Terms

| Term | Brief Definition |
|------|-----------------|
| Therapeutic communication | A purposeful, goal-directed exchange that promotes patient understanding, trust, and well-being |
| Active listening | Fully attending to a speaker — verbal, nonverbal, and emotional content — without interruption |
| Orientation phase | The first phase of the nurse-patient relationship in which trust and goals are established |
| Working phase | The middle phase of the nurse-patient relationship in which therapeutic goals are pursued |
| Termination phase | The concluding phase in which the therapeutic relationship is formally and purposefully ended |
| Open-ended question | A question that cannot be answered with "yes" or "no," inviting elaboration |
| Reflection | Restating or mirroring the patient's words or feelings to demonstrate understanding |
| Motivational interviewing | A patient-centered counseling style designed to elicit intrinsic motivation for behavior change |
| OARS | Core motivational interviewing skills: Open questions, Affirmations, Reflections, Summaries |
| SBAR | Structured handoff communication tool: Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation |
| I-PASS | Handoff mnemonic: Illness severity, Patient summary, Action list, Situation awareness, Synthesis |
| Non-therapeutic communication | Patterns that block trust or understanding (e.g., false reassurance, giving advice, changing the subject) |
| Proxemics | The study of personal space and how spatial distance affects communication |
| Kinesics | Body language, including gestures, posture, facial expressions, and eye contact |
| Health literacy | The degree to which individuals can obtain, process, and understand basic health information |

### Section 4.2 — Verbal and Nonverbal Communication

**Purpose:** Help students recognize that most clinical meaning is conveyed nonverbally; train congruence between words and body language.

#### 4.2.1 — Characteristics of Effective Verbal Communication
- Clarity, simplicity, brevity, and timing (Kozier & Erb's "CSBT" principles)
- Tone, pace, volume, and inflection
- Avoiding jargon with patients vs. appropriate clinical language with colleagues

#### 4.2.2 — Nonverbal Communication Domains
- **Kinesics:** facial expressions, eye contact, gestures, posture
- **Proxemics:** intimate (0–18 in.), personal (1.5–4 ft.), social (4–12 ft.), public zones
- **Haptics:** therapeutic touch — cultural considerations and patient consent
- **Paralanguage:** pitch, rate, volume, pauses, sighs
- Figure placeholder: [FIGURE 4.2 — Proxemics zones in clinical nursing interaction]

#### 4.2.3 — Congruence and Incongruence
- When verbal and nonverbal messages conflict, patients believe the nonverbal
- Clinical example: "Everything is fine" said with tense posture and avoidance of eye contact
- Self-awareness exercise for students

### Section 4.3 — The Nurse-Patient Relationship: Phases and Boundaries

**Purpose:** Frame therapeutic communication within a professional, structured relationship model.

#### 4.3.1 — Hildegard Peplau's Interpersonal Theory
- Overview of Peplau's contribution as foundation for therapeutic relationship framework
- Connection to Chapter 1 content on nursing theory

#### 4.3.2 — Orientation Phase
- Establishing rapport and trust
- Introducing self, role, and purpose of the interaction
- Assessing communication needs, language preferences, and literacy level
- Setting the agenda collaboratively

#### 4.3.3 — Working Phase
- Active problem-solving and goal-directed interaction
- Nurse roles: resource person, counselor, teacher, advocate
- Transference and countertransference: recognizing and managing personal reactions

#### 4.3.4 — Termination Phase
- Planned endings: preparing patients for discharge, care transitions, or end of shift
- Unplanned endings: patient deterioration, transfer, death
- Self-evaluation: reflecting on therapeutic effectiveness

#### 4.3.5 — Professional Boundaries
- Therapeutic vs. social relationship: key distinctions
- Boundary crossings vs. boundary violations
- Social media and digital communication: maintaining professional limits
- Callout: **Professional Practice** — ANA position on social media and nursing

### Section 4.4 — Therapeutic Communication Techniques

**Purpose:** Build a practical toolkit of evidence-based techniques students can apply immediately.

#### 4.4.1 — Active Listening
- SOLER/SURETY model (Egan's attending behaviors): Squarely face, Open posture, Lean toward, Eye contact, Relaxed
- Attending vs. pretending to listen
- Minimizing interruptions

#### 4.4.2 — Verbal Therapeutic Techniques (with clinical examples for each)
- **Open-ended questions:** "Tell me more about what you're experiencing."
- **Focused questions:** narrowing without leading
- **Reflection:** parroting and paraphrasing
- **Clarification:** "When you say it hurts, can you describe what that feels like?"
- **Restatement:** repeating key words to encourage elaboration
- **Summarizing:** condensing and verifying understanding
- **Acknowledging feelings:** naming emotions without judgment
- **Silence:** therapeutic use of pauses; cultural context
- **Sharing observations:** "I notice you seem tense today."
- **Giving information:** clear, plain-language health teaching
- **Confrontation (caring):** addressing inconsistencies between behavior and stated goals
- Figure placeholder: [FIGURE 4.3 — Comparison table: therapeutic vs. non-therapeutic techniques]

#### 4.4.3 — De-escalation Techniques
- Recognizing escalating agitation: behavioral cues
- LOWLINE approach and verbal de-escalation strategies
- Safety planning and when to involve security or colleagues

### Section 4.5 — Non-Therapeutic Communication: Barriers and Pitfalls

**Purpose:** Identify common communication errors nurses make and their impact on patient trust and outcomes.

#### 4.5.1 — Categories of Non-Therapeutic Communication
- **False reassurance:** "Everything will be fine." — removes patient's right to prepare
- **Giving advice:** "If I were you, I would..." — undermines autonomy
- **Changing the subject:** cutting off emotional expression
- **Closed questions:** limiting patient voice
- **Paternalism:** talking down or making decisions for the patient
- **Excessive self-disclosure:** shifting focus to the nurse
- **Stereotyping:** making assumptions based on age, culture, diagnosis
- **Defensive responses:** protecting the healthcare system instead of acknowledging concerns
- Table: common non-therapeutic statements paired with therapeutic alternatives

#### 4.5.2 — Environmental and Systemic Barriers
- Noise, interruptions, lack of privacy
- Time pressure and documentation burden
- Health literacy mismatch
- Language differences and absent interpreter services

#### 4.5.3 — Communication Failure and Patient Safety
- JCAHO data: communication as leading root cause of sentinel events
- Examples: hand-off failures, incomplete informed consent, missed escalation
- Connection to SBAR (preview of Section 4.8)

### Section 4.6 — Motivational Interviewing: The OARS Technique

**Purpose:** Equip students with a structured, patient-centered counseling approach for behavior change.

#### 4.6.1 — Foundations of Motivational Interviewing (MI)
- Miller & Rollnick's definition and evidence base
- Spirit of MI: partnership, acceptance, compassion, evocation (PACE)
- Stages of Change (Prochaska & DiClemente): precontemplation through maintenance

#### 4.6.2 — The OARS Core Skills
- **Open questions:** eliciting patient's perspective and motivation
- **Affirmations:** recognizing strengths and efforts, not empty praise
- **Reflections:** simple and complex; double-sided reflection
- **Summaries:** collecting, linking, and transitioning

#### 4.6.3 — Change Talk vs. Sustain Talk
- Recognizing DARN-C (Desire, Ability, Reason, Need, Commitment) as indicators of readiness
- Rolling with resistance rather than confronting it
- Brief MI in time-limited clinical encounters

#### 4.6.4 — Clinical Applications
- Tobacco cessation
- Medication adherence
- Dietary changes and physical activity
- Substance use and mental health conversations
- Callout: **Evidence-Based Practice Spotlight** — Cochrane reviews on MI effectiveness in nursing

### Section 4.8 — Interdisciplinary Communication: SBAR and I-PASS Handoff Tools

**Purpose:** Translate therapeutic communication principles to the interdisciplinary team context and patient safety.

#### 4.8.1 — The SBAR Framework
- Origin in military and aviation, adapted for healthcare (Institute for Healthcare Improvement)
- S — Situation: current, concise statement of the problem
- B — Background: relevant history and context
- A — Assessment: nurse's clinical judgment of what is happening
- R — Recommendation: what the nurse is requesting or proposing
- Scripted clinical example: nurse calling physician about deteriorating patient
- Callout: **Clinical Tip** — Practice SBAR before picking up the phone

#### 4.8.2 — The I-PASS Handoff Tool
- Evidence base: I-PASS study (Starmer et al., 2014)
- I — Illness severity (stable, watcher, unstable)
- P — Patient summary: diagnosis, clinical trajectory
- A — Action list: pending tasks and responsible party
- S — Situation awareness and contingency planning
- S — Synthesis by receiver (verbal read-back confirming understanding)
- Comparison: SBAR (urgent, vertical) vs. I-PASS (routine, horizontal handoffs)

#### 4.8.3 — Escalating Concerns: Chain of Command
- When to escalate beyond the bedside provider
- IESB (Identify, Explain, Seek, Bring) escalation model
- Rapid Response Team activation criteria
- Documentation requirements after an escalation call

### Section 4.9 — Documentation Standards: Legal Pitfalls and EHR Best Practices

**Purpose:** Tie communication principles to the permanent legal and clinical record.

#### 4.9.1 — Principles of Nursing Documentation
- Accuracy, completeness, timeliness, and objectivity (FACT acronym: Factual, Accurate, Complete, Timely)
- Objective vs. subjective language: "Patient states" vs. "Patient appears"
- The rule: if it wasn't documented, it wasn't done

#### 4.9.2 — Legal Implications of Documentation
- Documentation as the primary evidence in malpractice litigation
- Late entries and addenda: correct process and prohibited practices (never alter or delete)
- Incident reports: what goes in the chart vs. what stays in the report
- Copy-forward errors in EHRs: cloned documentation risks

#### 4.9.3 — EHR Communication Features
- Secure messaging, flags, and alerts
- HIPAA considerations for electronic communication (email, text, patient portal)
- Verbal order documentation requirements (CPOE vs. transcription)
- Interdisciplinary notes: purpose and format

#### 4.9.4 — Bedside Shift Report
- Evidence base for bedside reporting (patient satisfaction, safety, nurse accountability)
- Involving the patient: inviting questions, reviewing the care plan together
- Callout: **Quality Improvement** — Bedside shift report and HCAHPS scores

## Case Study Concept

**Title:** "Lost in Translation"

**Setting:** Medical-surgical unit, tertiary care hospital

**Scenario Overview:**  
Maria, a 72-year-old Spanish-speaking woman with limited English proficiency, is admitted for management of newly diagnosed type 2 diabetes. Her adult son insists on interpreting for her "to save time." The night nurse uses a phone-based interpreter during her initial assessment and discovers that Maria has significant fears about insulin that her son had not communicated. The case unfolds across three scenes: admission assessment, diabetes education session, and morning hand-off to the day nurse.

**Discussion Questions (draft):**
1. Why is using a family member as an interpreter problematic even when the patient consents, and what are the legal and ethical obligations of the nurse?
2. Identify two therapeutic communication techniques the night nurse used during the assessment. How did each technique contribute to Maria's disclosure of her concerns about insulin?
3. The day nurse receives an SBAR hand-off that omits Maria's language needs. Using the I-PASS format, how would you reconstruct a complete and safe hand-off?
4. How does the principle of patient advocacy intersect with therapeutic communication in this scenario? What actions should the nurse take if systemic barriers (e.g., no interpreter available) prevent language access?
