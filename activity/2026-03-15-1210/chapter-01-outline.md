## Rationale for Choosing This Chapter First

Chapter 1 is the recommended first deliverable for the following reasons:

1. **No prerequisites.** It depends on no other chapter and can be drafted immediately.
2. **Sets the professional identity frame.** Every subsequent chapter is built on the assumption that the student understands what nursing *is*, where it came from, and why its historical development matters. This chapter provides that foundation.
3. **Low clinical complexity.** It introduces narrative, historical, and conceptual content rather than clinical procedures, making it accessible to begin drafting before clinical chapters are outlined.
4. **High motivational value.** Students who encounter a compelling opening chapter are more engaged throughout the rest of the text.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

1. Summarize key milestones in the history of nursing from ancient times to the present.
2. Explain Florence Nightingale's foundational contributions to modern nursing practice and education.
3. Describe the development of nursing education, licensure, and professional organizations in the United States.
4. Analyze nursing's evolving role in wartime, public health, and social movements.
5. Identify the range of contemporary nursing roles, settings, and specialties.
6. Explain the metaparadigm of nursing and apply it to a clinical example.
7. Compare and contrast selected nursing theories and their implications for practice.
8. Discuss emerging trends shaping the future of nursing.

## Key Terms

| Term | Brief Definition |
|------|-----------------|
| Nursing | The protection, promotion, and optimization of health and abilities; prevention of illness and injury; alleviation of suffering through diagnosis and treatment of human response; and advocacy in the care of individuals, families, communities, and populations (ANA, 2021) |
| Metaparadigm | The four central concepts of nursing: person, environment, health, and nursing |
| Nursing theory | A systematic framework of concepts, definitions, and propositions that organizes and guides nursing practice and research |
| Evidence-based practice (EBP) | The integration of best research evidence, clinical expertise, and patient values to guide clinical decision-making |
| QSEN | Quality and Safety Education for Nurses; a national initiative defining the knowledge, skills, and attitudes nurses need to improve quality and safety |
| Scope of practice | The range of activities that a licensed nurse is legally authorized to perform |
| Professional socialization | The process by which individuals acquire the values, norms, and behaviors of the nursing profession |
| Nightingale Pledge | A modified version of the Hippocratic Oath for nurses, expressing commitment to professional and ethical practice |
| American Nurses Association (ANA) | The professional organization representing the interests of the nation's registered nurses |
| NCLEX-RN | National Council Licensure Examination for Registered Nurses; the standardized exam required for licensure |

### Section 1.1 — Ancient Healing Practices and Early Nursing Traditions (Approximately 3 pages)

**1.1.1 Healing in the Ancient World**

- Care of the sick in ancient Egypt, Greece, Rome, and the Arab world
- The role of religious orders and monasteries in caring for the ill
- Wound care, herbal remedies, and the origins of bedside caregiving
- How gender and social class shaped who provided care

**1.1.2 Medieval and Pre-Modern Nursing**

- The Catholic nursing orders (e.g., Order of the Holy Spirit, Augustinian nuns)
- The Crusades and military nursing
- Plague nursing and the emergence of secular caregivers
- Wet nursing, midwifery, and domestic care: nursing before the profession existed

**1.1.3 Nursing in the 18th and Early 19th Centuries**

- The "Sairey Gamp" problem: nursing's pre-Nightingale reputation
- Early American nursing: widows, religious women, and enslaved people forced to care
- The preconditions that made nursing reform necessary

---

### Section 1.2 — Florence Nightingale and the Birth of Modern Nursing (Approximately 4 pages)

**1.2.1 Nightingale's Life and Context**

- Background, education, and early calling
- The Victorian context: women's roles, social reform movements, and public health
- Nightingale's statistical work and the rose diagram (polar area chart)

**1.2.2 The Crimean War (1854–1856): Nursing's Turning Point**

- Conditions at Scutari before Nightingale's arrival
- The 38 nurses Nightingale brought to the Barrack Hospital
- Sanitary reforms and the dramatic reduction in mortality
- Media coverage and the creation of the "Lady with the Lamp" image

**1.2.3 The Nightingale Principles**

- *Notes on Nursing* (1859): the first nursing text and its core ideas
- Ventilation, cleanliness, light, warmth, and quiet as therapeutic environments
- The Nightingale Pledge
- Establishment of the Nightingale Training School at St Thomas's Hospital (1860)

**1.2.4 Nightingale's Legacy and Limitations**

- Enduring contributions to environmental theory, nursing education, and data-driven healthcare
- Critiques: race, class, and the exclusion of non-white and working-class women

---

### Section 1.3 — Development of Nursing Education, Licensure, and Professional Organizations (Approximately 4 pages)

**1.3.1 Early Nursing Schools in the United States**

- The Bellevue Training School (1873) and the Nightingale model in America
- The role of the Civil War in accelerating nursing's development
- Mary Mahoney: the first licensed Black professional nurse in the United States (1879)
- Hospital-based diploma programs and their strengths and limitations

**1.3.2 The Struggle for Licensure and Professional Standards**

- The first state nursing practice acts (North Carolina, 1903)
- The Goldmark Report (1923) and the push for university-based education
- The Brown Report (1948): *Nursing for the Future*
- The National League for Nursing (NLN) and accreditation

**1.3.3 The Rise of the Registered Nurse**

- Transition from diploma to associate degree to baccalaureate preparation
- The NCLEX-RN: development and current form
- The Doctor of Nursing Practice (DNP) and the movement toward doctoral preparation for advanced practice

**1.3.4 Professional Organizations and Collective Voice**

- The American Nurses Association (ANA): structure, purpose, and advocacy
- The National Student Nurses Association (NSNA)
- Specialty organizations (AACN, ONS, AWHONN, APNA, etc.)
- International Council of Nurses (ICN) and global nursing standards

### Section 1.4 — Nursing's Role in War, Public Health, and Social Movements (Approximately 3 pages)

**1.4.1 Military Nursing**

- Clara Barton and the American Civil War
- The Spanish-American War and the Army Nurse Corps (1901)
- World War I: nursing under fire
- World War II: the Army and Navy nurse corps, the Cadet Nurse Corps, and integration
- Korea, Vietnam, and contemporary military nursing

**1.4.2 Public Health Nursing**

- Lillian Wald and the Henry Street Settlement (1893)
- School nursing and the prevention of infectious disease
- The Frontier Nursing Service and rural healthcare
- Public health nursing's influence on community and population health practice today

**1.4.3 Nursing, Civil Rights, and Social Movements**

- Racial segregation in nursing: the National Association of Colored Graduate Nurses (NACGN)
- The integration of the armed forces and its impact on nursing
- Nursing and the women's rights movement
- Contemporary DEI efforts in nursing education and workforce

**1.5.1 Levels of Nursing Practice**

- Acute care: hospitals, ICUs, emergency departments

- Research, education, and policy

**1.5.3 Nursing Specialties**

- Overview of major specialties: medical-surgical, critical care, pediatrics, obstetrics, oncology, psychiatric, perioperative, emergency
- Specialty certification and its value

**1.5.4 The Changing Nursing Workforce**

- Current nursing shortage: causes and projections
- Workforce diversity: gender, race/ethnicity, and international nurses
- Burnout, retention, and workplace well-being

### Section 1.6 — Nursing Theory and Conceptual Models (Approximately 4 pages)

**1.6.1 Why Nursing Theory Matters**

- The relationship between theory, research, and practice
- Theory as a guide for clinical decision-making

**1.6.2 Selected Nursing Theorists and Their Core Ideas**

| Theorist | Theory | Core Concept |
|----------|--------|--------------|
| Florence Nightingale | Environmental Theory | Manipulating the environment to support healing |
| Virginia Henderson | Need Theory | 14 basic needs; the nurse helps the patient achieve independence |
| Dorothea Orem | Self-Care Deficit Theory | Nursing acts when the patient cannot perform self-care |
| Callista Roy | Adaptation Model | Persons adapt to stimuli; nursing supports adaptive responses |
| Jean Watson | Theory of Human Caring | Caring as the moral ideal and core of nursing |
| Madeleine Leininger | Culture Care Theory | Culturally congruent care is essential to health and well-being |
| Betty Neuman | Systems Model | The client is a system; nursing protects against stressors |
| Patricia Benner | Novice to Expert | Nurses develop expertise through stages of clinical experience |

**1.6.3 The Metaparadigm of Nursing**

- *Person:* the recipient of care, viewed holistically
- *Environment:* all internal and external conditions affecting the person
- *Health:* a dynamic state across a continuum
- *Nursing:* the actions taken to promote health and prevent or treat illness

**1.6.4 Applying Theory in Practice**

- Brief clinical scenario: how Orem's self-care deficit theory guides a discharge teaching plan
- Choosing a theoretical framework: questions to ask

### Case Study: Clinical Scenario

**Title:** *Meeting Margaret: A Student's First Day on the Unit*

**Setting:** A busy medical-surgical unit in a community hospital.

**Scenario:** A first-semester nursing student, Jordan, arrives for clinical orientation. Jordan's preceptor, an experienced RN named Margaret, explains that she became a nurse 25 years ago because of a hospitalization she experienced as a child. As Margaret orients Jordan to the unit, she describes her practice philosophy, how the profession has changed, and how she uses the nursing process and caring theory in everyday interactions with patients.

**Discussion Questions:**
1. How do Margaret's experiences reflect the concept of professional socialization?
2. Which nursing theorist's work is most reflected in Margaret's description of her practice? Justify your answer.
3. How might Jordan's understanding of nursing history influence the type of nurse Jordan becomes?
4. Margaret mentions that "the nurse's job isn't just tasks — it's about the whole person." Which metaparadigm concept does this reflect?
