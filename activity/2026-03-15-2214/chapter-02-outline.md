## Learning Objectives (Draft)

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

1. Explain the purpose and authority of the Nurse Practice Act and identify how it defines scope of practice in a student's state.
2. Apply the Five Rights of Delegation to clinical scenarios, distinguishing tasks that may be delegated from those that may not.
3. Differentiate negligence from malpractice and describe the nurse's professional liability in each.
4. Describe HIPAA protections, identify permissible disclosures, and apply privacy principles to EHR documentation.
5. Identify mandatory reporting obligations, including abuse, communicable disease, and sentinel events.
6. Analyze the ethical and legal risks of professional boundary violations and social media misconduct.
7. Distinguish the four bioethical principles (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice) and apply them to nursing practice dilemmas.
8. Describe the provisions of the ANA Code of Ethics for Nurses and explain their implications for daily nursing practice.
9. Apply an ethical decision-making framework to resolve a nursing practice dilemma.
10. Define moral distress and moral resilience, and identify strategies for sustaining ethical practice under institutional pressure.

## Key Terms (Draft — Minimum 15)

| Term | Brief Definition |
|------|-----------------|
| Nurse Practice Act (NPA) | State legislation defining the legal scope and standards of nursing practice |
| Scope of practice | The procedures, actions, and processes a nurse is permitted to perform based on education, licensure, and law |
| Delegation | Transferring responsibility for a nursing task to another competent individual while retaining accountability |
| Five Rights of Delegation | Framework: right task, right circumstance, right person, right direction/communication, right supervision |
| Negligence | Failure to exercise the standard of care a reasonable person would provide, resulting in harm |
| Malpractice | Professional negligence — failure to meet the standard of care expected of a licensed professional |
| HIPAA | Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act; federal law protecting patient health information |
| Protected health information (PHI) | Any individually identifiable health information held or transmitted by a covered entity |
| Mandatory reporting | Legal obligation to report specific events (abuse, certain diseases, sentinel events) to authorities |
| Professional boundary | The space between a nurse's power and a patient's vulnerability that protects the therapeutic relationship |
| Autonomy | The patient's right to make informed decisions about their own care |
| Beneficence | The ethical obligation to act in the patient's best interest |
| Non-maleficence | The obligation to do no harm |
| Justice | Fair and equitable distribution of healthcare resources and treatment |
| ANA Code of Ethics | The nine-provision ethical framework guiding nursing practice, published by the American Nurses Association |
| Moral distress | The distress that arises when a nurse knows the ethically correct action but is constrained from taking it |
| Moral resilience | The capacity to sustain ethical practice and restore well-being in the face of moral adversity |
| Ethics committee | A multidisciplinary body providing guidance on complex ethical decisions in clinical settings |
| Health equity | The principle that every person should have a fair opportunity to attain their full health potential |
| Continuing education (CE) | Ongoing learning required to maintain nursing licensure and competence |

### Case Study: "The Scope Dilemma"

**Setting:** Medical-surgical unit, community hospital

**Scene 1:** Aisha, a newly licensed RN, is asked by a time-pressured charge nurse to perform a complex wound irrigation procedure she has never been trained on. The charge nurse says, "Just follow the order — everyone on this unit does it."

**Scene 2:** Aisha reviews the policy manual, realizes the procedure requires additional competency validation, and speaks with the clinical educator. The charge nurse becomes hostile and threatens a negative evaluation.

**Scene 3:** Aisha documents the interaction, completes an incident report, and requests a meeting with the nurse manager. The nurse manager reviews the policy and validates Aisha's position.

**Discussion Questions:**
1. What Nurse Practice Act principles apply to Aisha's situation, and what should she have considered before accepting the assignment?
2. How do the concepts of delegation and scope of practice interact in Scene 1?
3. What ethical principles and ANA Code provisions support Aisha's decision to escalate in Scene 3?
4. How might moral distress manifest for Aisha following this event, and what institutional strategies could help prevent it in the future?
