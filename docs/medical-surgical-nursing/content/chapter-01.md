## Key Terms

| Term | Brief Definition |
|------|-----------------|
| **Medical-surgical nursing** | A specialty area of nursing practice focused on the care of adult patients experiencing acute and chronic medical conditions, as well as those requiring surgical intervention |
| **Scope of practice** | The range of activities, procedures, and responsibilities that a licensed nurse is legally and professionally authorized to perform |
| **Interprofessional collaborative practice (IPCP)** | A model of healthcare delivery in which professionals from multiple disciplines work together, sharing knowledge and responsibilities to achieve optimal patient outcomes |
| **IPEC** | Interprofessional Education Collaborative; the organization that defines core competencies for interprofessional collaborative practice in the United States |
| **TeamSTEPPS** | Team Strategies and Tools to Enhance Performance and Patient Safety; an evidence-based teamwork training program developed by AHRQ and the Department of Defense |
| **AMSN** | Academy of Medical-Surgical Nurses; the professional membership organization dedicated to medical-surgical nursing |
| **MSNCB** | Medical-Surgical Nursing Certification Board; the credentialing body that administers the CMSRN and PCCN certification examinations |
| **CMSRN** | Certified Medical-Surgical Registered Nurse; the specialty certification credential awarded by the MSNCB |
| **Patient acuity** | A measure of the severity of a patient's illness and the complexity of nursing care required |
| **Nurse-to-patient ratio** | The number of patients assigned to one nurse during a given shift; a key determinant of patient safety and nurse workload |
| **Delegation** | The transfer of responsibility for performing a task — but not accountability — from a registered nurse to another nursing team member within the scope of that person's role |
| **Rapid response team (RRT)** | An interprofessional team that responds to deteriorating patients outside the ICU to prevent failure to rescue |
| **Failure to rescue** | A nurse-sensitive quality indicator defined as the inability to prevent a patient's death or serious harm from a complication that was potentially detectable and treatable |
| **Nursing-sensitive indicator** | A measurable aspect of nursing care — such as fall rate, pressure injury incidence, or restraint use — that reflects the quality and safety of nursing practice |
| **Continuing education (CE)** | Formal learning activities beyond initial nursing education that maintain, develop, or expand professional competence |
| **Evidence-based practice (EBP)** | The integration of best research evidence, clinical expertise, and patient values and preferences to guide clinical decision-making |

## Introduction

Consider a Tuesday morning on a 32-bed medical-surgical unit at a mid-sized community hospital. In Room 408, a 67-year-old woman with type 2 diabetes and newly diagnosed heart failure is being titrated off intravenous diuretics in anticipation of discharge. Across the hall in Room 412, a 52-year-old construction worker is recovering from an emergency appendectomy performed eighteen hours earlier, requiring pain assessment every two hours and wound site monitoring. Two doors down, a 79-year-old man with dementia, admitted for a urinary tract infection, has pulled out his peripheral IV line for the third time this shift. At the nursing station, the charge nurse is reviewing a SBAR communication from a night-shift colleague about a patient in Room 418 whose oxygen saturation has been trending downward and who will need a rapid-response evaluation before 0800. A certified nursing assistant approaches with vital signs that need to be reviewed and countersigned.

This is the world of medical-surgical nursing. It is not the controlled, procedure-specific environment of the operating suite, nor the continuous monitoring of the intensive care unit. It is a dynamic, high-volume, high-acuity environment that demands from nurses an extraordinary breadth of clinical knowledge, sharp prioritization skills, effective communication, and the professional confidence to act decisively on behalf of patients who cannot always advocate for themselves.

Medical-surgical nursing is the largest single nursing specialty in the United States, employing hundreds of thousands of registered nurses across hospital wards, ambulatory surgery centers, outpatient infusion suites, and long-term acute care facilities. It is frequently the entry point for new graduate nurses and, for many, a lifelong specialty career defined by constant intellectual challenge and the opportunity to build deep, sustained therapeutic relationships with patients navigating some of the most difficult experiences of their lives.

This chapter establishes the foundation for everything that follows in this textbook. Section 1.1 defines medical-surgical nursing and its scope of practice. Section 1.2 traces the historical forces that shaped the specialty. Section 1.3 describes the core roles and competencies required of the medical-surgical nurse. Section 1.4 explores interprofessional collaborative practice — the team-based model of care delivery that characterizes contemporary medical-surgical settings. Section 1.5 surveys the current landscape: the trends in acuity, staffing, and technology that define the specialty today. Section 1.6 introduces the professional organizations, certification pathways, and career development resources available to medical-surgical nurses at every stage of their careers.

## Section 1.1 — Scope and Definition of Medical-Surgical Nursing

### 1.1.1 Defining the Specialty

Medical-surgical nursing is formally defined as the practice of nursing with adult patients who experience known or predicted physiologic alterations with various levels of diagnosed conditions (Academy of Medical-Surgical Nurses [AMSN], 2020). More broadly, it is the nursing specialty dedicated to the care of adult patients experiencing acute illness, chronic disease exacerbation, or recovery from surgery or invasive procedures. The Academy of Medical-Surgical Nurses and the Medical-Surgical Nursing Certification Board collaboratively publish the *Medical-Surgical Nursing: Scope and Standards of Practice*, which delineates the professional responsibilities, competencies, and accountability structures that define the specialty.

The scope of medical-surgical nursing is deliberately broad. Unlike subspecialties such as cardiac care or neurology, which focus on a defined organ system or patient population, medical-surgical nursing encompasses virtually the entire range of adult health conditions. A medical-surgical nurse must be prepared to provide safe, competent care to a patient with pneumonia in the same shift as a patient recovering from orthopedic surgery and a patient in the midst of a diabetic crisis — conditions requiring completely different pathophysiologic knowledge, pharmacologic understanding, and nursing interventions. This breadth is simultaneously the greatest intellectual demand and the greatest professional reward of the specialty.

The *Nursing: Scope and Standards of Practice* (American Nurses Association [ANA], 2021) provides the overarching framework within which medical-surgical nursing practice is situated. The ANA defines the registered nurse's scope of practice in terms of assessment, diagnosis, outcomes identification, planning, implementation, and evaluation — the nursing process — and articulates standards of professional performance that include ethics, advocacy, education, evidence-based practice, quality of practice, communication, leadership, collaboration, and resource utilization. The specialty-specific AMSN standards build on this foundation, specifying the competencies required of nurses who practice in medical-surgical settings.

A critical feature of medical-surgical nursing's scope is its emphasis on **holistic care** — the recognition that a patient's physical illness is inseparable from their psychological experience, social context, spiritual concerns, and functional status. The medical-surgical nurse does not simply manage a disease or a set of clinical parameters. The nurse cares for a whole person who has a life, a family, fears, values, and goals that must be understood and respected to deliver care that is not merely technically correct but genuinely beneficial.

> **Professional Tip: Understanding Scope of Practice**
> Every registered nurse is responsible for knowing the scope of practice defined by their state's Nurse Practice Act (NPA). While the AMSN scope and standards document describes specialty-level practice, the NPA is the legal document that defines what a nurse in a given state is authorized to do. When in doubt about whether an intervention is within your scope of practice, consult your institution's policy, your state NPA, and a nurse leader or educator.

### 1.1.2 Settings and Patient Populations

The most familiar setting for medical-surgical nursing is the general inpatient hospital ward. These units, typically ranging from 24 to 48 beds, serve as the primary destination for adult patients admitted through the emergency department, transferred from the ICU during step-down recovery, or admitted directly from physician offices or ambulatory surgical centers. Medical-surgical floors serve the full spectrum of adult ages — from young adults in their twenties to centenarians — and the full spectrum of medical diagnoses.

However, medical-surgical nursing has expanded well beyond the hospital inpatient unit. Contemporary medical-surgical nurses practice in:

- **Ambulatory surgery centers (ASCs)**: Providing preoperative preparation and postoperative recovery care for patients undergoing same-day procedures.
- **Outpatient infusion centers**: Administering chemotherapy, biologics, blood products, and intravenous antibiotics to patients who do not require hospitalization.
- **Long-term acute care hospitals (LTACHs)**: Caring for medically complex patients who require extended inpatient care but do not meet the acuity criteria for continued ICU or acute hospital admission.
- **Rehabilitation units**: Supporting patients recovering from surgery, stroke, or trauma as they regain functional independence.
- **Skilled nursing facilities (SNFs)**: Providing post-acute care for patients transitioning from the hospital to home.
- **Home health agencies**: Delivering hospital-level nursing interventions — wound care, IV therapy, cardiac monitoring — in the patient's home environment.

The patient populations served by medical-surgical nurses reflect the epidemiology of adult disease in the United States. The most common diagnoses encountered in medical-surgical settings include:

| Diagnosis Category | Common Examples |
|---|---|
| Cardiovascular | Heart failure, acute coronary syndromes, hypertension, peripheral vascular disease |
| Respiratory | Pneumonia, COPD exacerbation, pulmonary embolism, pleural effusion |
| Endocrine/Metabolic | Diabetes mellitus (types 1 and 2), thyroid disorders, adrenal insufficiency |
| Gastrointestinal | Bowel obstruction, GI bleeding, inflammatory bowel disease, hepatic failure |
| Genitourinary | Urinary tract infection, acute kidney injury, chronic kidney disease |
| Musculoskeletal | Fractures, post-arthroplasty recovery, osteomyelitis |

This diagnostic diversity underscores why medical-surgical nursing demands broad foundational knowledge and the cognitive flexibility to rapidly shift between clinical paradigms. A nurse who has spent the first four hours of a shift managing a patient with decompensated heart failure may spend the second four hours supporting a postoperative bowel resection patient — two conditions requiring dramatically different monitoring priorities, fluid management strategies, and patient education approaches.

## Section 1.2 — Historical Evolution of Medical-Surgical Nursing

### 1.2.1 Early Roots and the War Influence

Medical-surgical nursing as a recognizable specialty did not emerge in a vacuum. Its roots extend to the origins of organized nursing itself, and its development has been inseparable from the history of war, epidemics, industrialization, and social reform. To understand what medical-surgical nursing is today, it is necessary to understand the historical crucible in which it was forged.

The foundational figure of modern nursing, Florence Nightingale, was in many respects the prototype of the medical-surgical nurse. Her work at the British military hospital at Scutari during the Crimean War (1853–1856) was not psychiatric, pediatric, or community nursing — it was the acute care of adult men wounded in battle and suffering from infectious diseases, malnutrition, and surgical complications. Nightingale's innovations — rigorous environmental assessment, systematic data collection, targeted infection control, and evidence-based advocacy for systemic change — defined the intellectual and professional character of the specialty (Bostridge, 2008). Her insistence that nurses must understand the pathophysiology of disease in order to make environmental and clinical decisions marked medical-surgical nursing as an intellectually engaged specialty from its inception.

The American Civil War (1861–1865) accelerated the development of military nursing in the United States. Women such as Dorothea Dix, who served as Superintendent of Army Nurses, and Sojourner Truth organized nursing care for wounded soldiers under conditions of extraordinary logistical difficulty. Clara Barton — who later founded the American Red Cross — served as a nurse and supply coordinator in field hospitals, managing traumatic wounds, amputations, sepsis, and communicable diseases (Kalisch & Kalisch, 2004). The Civil War experience demonstrated that organized nursing under trained leadership dramatically improved surgical outcomes and survivability, establishing the professional and institutional case for trained nursing care in acute settings.

The Spanish-American War (1898) and World War I (1917–1918) continued to drive nursing professionalization. The Army Nurse Corps was established in 1901, followed by the Navy Nurse Corps in 1908 — the first official recognition by the U.S. federal government of nursing as an essential military and public health function. By World War II (1939–1945), military medical-surgical nurses were performing interventions — blood transfusions, debridement, triage, chest drainage — that approached what would later be recognized as advanced practice. The mass casualty experience of wartime surgery directly informed post-war standards for civilian surgical and medical nursing (Tomblin Murphy et al., 2012).

> **Historical Spotlight: Nurses in World War II**
> More than 59,000 American nurses served in the Army Nurse Corps during World War II, with thousands more in the Navy Nurse Corps. These nurses staffed field hospitals, evacuation hospitals, hospital ships, and transport aircraft, providing acute post-surgical care under combat conditions. Their experience with massive trauma, burns, wound infection, and shock resuscitation directly shaped the development of post-war medical-surgical nursing standards and the establishment of intensive care units in the 1950s and 1960s.

### 1.2.2 The 20th Century and Specialization

The second half of the twentieth century transformed medical-surgical nursing from a general ward-based practice into a recognized specialty with its own knowledge base, standards, and credentialing infrastructure. Several forces drove this transformation.

**Advances in medical technology** dramatically increased the complexity of acute care nursing. The introduction of mechanical ventilation in the 1950s, cardiac monitoring and defibrillation in the 1960s, and hemodialysis in the 1960s and 1970s created new categories of critically ill patients who required specialized nursing observation and intervention. This complexity drove the creation of dedicated intensive care units — a development that simultaneously elevated the acuity of remaining "general" medical-surgical patients, since the most severely ill patients were now concentrated in the ICU rather than distributed across medical and surgical floors.

**The establishment of professional infrastructure** in the 1960s and 1970s solidified medical-surgical nursing as a specialty. The Academy of Medical-Surgical Nurses was established in 1991, providing a professional home for the specialty. The Medical-Surgical Nursing Certification Board followed in 1992, offering the CMSRN credential — a formal recognition that the specialty required specialty-level knowledge, skill, and ongoing professional development.

**Changes in hospital financing** in the 1980s and 1990s — particularly the shift to diagnosis-related group (DRG)-based Medicare reimbursement — compressed hospital length of stay, increased patient turnover, and raised the average acuity of patients remaining on medical-surgical floors. Patients who would have remained hospitalized for two weeks in the 1970s were being discharged in four or five days by the mid-1990s, meaning that the patients present on any given medical-surgical unit at any given time were, on average, sicker and more complex than their historical counterparts (Kalisch et al., 2010).

**The evidence-based practice movement**, which gained momentum in the 1990s and accelerated in the 2000s, gave medical-surgical nursing a rigorous research foundation. The growing body of evidence on nursing-sensitive outcomes — connecting nurse staffing ratios, education levels, and practice environments to patient mortality, falls, pressure injuries, and healthcare-associated infections — established the professional and political case for adequately resourced medical-surgical nursing (Needleman et al., 2011; Aiken et al., 2014).

## Section 1.3 — Roles and Competencies of the Medical-Surgical Nurse

### 1.3.1 Core Nursing Roles

The medical-surgical nurse performs multiple overlapping roles simultaneously throughout every shift. Understanding these roles — and the professional values that animate them — is essential for new nurses developing their professional identity.

**Caregiver.** The caregiver role encompasses the direct clinical interventions that constitute the core of nursing work: physical assessment, medication administration, wound care, IV management, specimen collection, pain management, comfort measures, and basic hygiene and mobility assistance. Caregiving in medical-surgical nursing is not merely technical task execution; it is the vehicle through which nurses build therapeutic relationships, detect subtle changes in patient condition, and operationalize the nursing process. A nurse who changes a patient's abdominal wound dressing is simultaneously assessing the wound for signs of infection, palpating the abdomen for distension, observing the patient's comfort level, listening to concerns about discharge, and planning for the patient education session that will need to happen before tomorrow morning.

**Educator.** Medical-surgical nurses carry a heavy and consequential responsibility for patient and family education. Because hospitalization is often the time when patients and families are most motivated to learn — and most vulnerable to the consequences of ignorance — the nurse's teaching role is particularly high-stakes. Medical-surgical patient education encompasses disease process explanation, medication teaching (purpose, dose, side effects, schedule), self-monitoring skills (blood glucose measurement, blood pressure monitoring, wound assessment), activity restrictions, dietary modifications, when to seek urgent care, and follow-up appointment management. Effective patient education requires assessment of health literacy, use of plain language, teach-back methodology, and culturally sensitive communication.

**Advocate.** Patient advocacy is one of nursing's most ancient and enduring professional obligations. In the medical-surgical setting, advocacy takes many forms: ensuring that a patient's pain is adequately treated when the current analgesic order is insufficient; questioning a medication order that appears inconsistent with the patient's allergy list; representing a family's concern about discharge planning to the case manager; or escalating a safety concern about a patient's deteriorating mental status when other team members have not yet recognized the change. Effective advocacy requires clinical knowledge, professional courage, skilled communication, and a clear-eyed commitment to the patient's interests above personal or institutional comfort.

**Care Coordinator.** The contemporary medical-surgical nurse is a coordinator of care across a complex system of providers, services, and transitions. On any given shift, a nurse may need to coordinate diagnostic procedures (scheduling radiology, phlebotomy, respiratory therapy), communicate clinical updates to physicians and advanced practice providers, participate in discharge planning with case management and social work, arrange home health referrals, and facilitate family meetings. As the healthcare professional with the most continuous patient contact, the nurse is uniquely positioned — and professionally obligated — to ensure that the elements of the patient's care plan are integrated, communicated, and implemented effectively.

**Leader and Collaborator.** Even bedside staff nurses exercise leadership: they lead the patient care team (which may include nursing assistants, student nurses, and support staff) on their assigned patients, they serve as charge nurse or resource nurse as they gain experience, and they contribute to unit-level quality improvement and practice change initiatives. Leadership in nursing is not a role reserved for those with administrative titles — it is a professional expectation embedded in every nurse's daily practice.

### 1.3.2 Competencies and the AMSN Standards

The AMSN *Medical-Surgical Nursing: Scope and Standards of Practice* (2020) articulates the competencies expected of medical-surgical nurses at both the registered nurse (RN) level and the certified medical-surgical registered nurse (CMSRN) level. These competencies are organized around the nursing process (assessment, diagnosis, outcomes identification, planning, implementation, evaluation) and a set of professional performance standards.

Competencies specific to the medical-surgical specialty include:

- **Clinical assessment and reasoning**: Conducting systematic, head-to-toe assessments; recognizing normal and abnormal findings across multiple body systems; applying clinical judgment to prioritize assessment data.
- **Pharmacologic management**: Safely administering a wide range of medications, including high-alert drugs (anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, electrolyte replacements); recognizing adverse effects; educating patients about their medications.
- **Wound and skin integrity management**: Assessing and staging pressure injuries; performing complex wound care; applying evidence-based protocols for pressure injury prevention.
- **Pain management**: Conducting valid pain assessments; implementing multimodal pain management strategies; monitoring for opioid-related adverse effects; distinguishing acute from chronic pain.
- **Fluid and electrolyte management**: Monitoring intake and output; recognizing clinical manifestations of fluid and electrolyte imbalances; administering and monitoring IV therapy.
- **Patient and family education**: Assessing learning needs and barriers; implementing teach-back; adapting teaching strategies to health literacy level and cultural context.
- **Care transitions**: Participating in safe handoffs using standardized communication tools (SBAR, I-PASS); coordinating discharge planning; providing transition-of-care instructions.
- **Delegation and supervision**: Appropriately delegating tasks to nursing assistants and unlicensed assistive personnel in accordance with state scope of practice regulations and institutional policy; supervising delegated care.

> **Evidence-Based Practice: Nurse Education and Patient Outcomes**
> A landmark multi-country study by Aiken et al. (2014) found that each 10-percentage-point increase in the proportion of hospital nurses with a bachelor's degree was associated with a 7% decrease in patient mortality. Higher nurse education levels were also associated with decreased odds of 30-day mortality and failure to rescue. These findings underscore the importance of educational advancement for medical-surgical nurses and support policies that incentivize BSN preparation and ongoing graduate education.

## Section 1.4 — Interprofessional Collaborative Practice

### 1.4.1 The Interprofessional Team in Medical-Surgical Settings

Modern medical-surgical care is inherently team-based. No single professional — regardless of training, experience, or authority — possesses all the knowledge, skills, and resources required to meet the complex, multidimensional needs of a hospitalized adult patient. The recognition of this reality has driven a global movement toward **interprofessional collaborative practice (IPCP)**: a model of care delivery in which professionals from two or more disciplines work together to provide care, recognizing each other's complementary expertise, communicating transparently, and sharing responsibility for outcomes (World Health Organization, 2010).

On a typical medical-surgical unit, the interprofessional team may include:

- **Registered nurses (RNs)**: Providing 24/7 continuous care, assessment, monitoring, and coordination
- **Physicians (MDs/DOs)**: Diagnosing conditions, prescribing treatments, performing procedures
- **Advanced practice registered nurses (APRNs)**: Nurse practitioners and clinical nurse specialists who may serve as primary providers or consultants
- **Physician assistants (PAs)**: Providing medical management under physician supervision
- **Pharmacists**: Reviewing medication orders, identifying drug interactions, providing patient and nurse education about medications
- **Physical therapists (PTs)**: Restoring mobility, strength, and functional independence
- **Occupational therapists (OTs)**: Addressing activities of daily living and adaptive equipment needs
- **Speech-language pathologists (SLPs)**: Evaluating and managing dysphagia and communication disorders
- **Respiratory therapists (RTs)**: Managing ventilatory support, oxygen therapy, and pulmonary rehabilitation
- **Registered dietitians (RDs)**: Assessing nutritional status and prescribing therapeutic diets and enteral/parenteral nutrition
- **Social workers**: Coordinating community resources, addressing social determinants of health, facilitating discharge planning
- **Case managers**: Coordinating care transitions, managing length of stay, ensuring post-acute services are arranged
- **Chaplains/Spiritual care providers**: Addressing spiritual and existential dimensions of illness and dying
- **Nursing assistants (NAs) / Patient care technicians (PCTs)**: Providing direct patient care under RN supervision

The registered nurse plays a unique and central role in this interprofessional team. Because the nurse is the only team member present at the bedside around the clock, the nurse functions as the integrating hub of the team: receiving information from every direction, synthesizing it in the context of continuous patient observation, and distributing it to the appropriate team members. The nurse is frequently the first to detect a patient's clinical deterioration and the professional who initiates the response, whether that means calling the physician, activating the rapid response team, or implementing a standing order protocol.

### 1.4.2 IPEC Core Competencies and TeamSTEPPS

The **Interprofessional Education Collaborative (IPEC)** has articulated four core competency domains that define effective interprofessional collaborative practice (IPEC, 2023):

1. **Values/Ethics for Interprofessional Practice**: Placing the patient and population at the center of healthcare delivery; respecting the dignity, privacy, and preferences of patients and families; maintaining honesty and integrity in all professional interactions; respecting the unique cultures, values, roles, and responsibilities of other health professions.

2. **Roles and Responsibilities**: Understanding one's own scope of practice and the scopes of practice of other team members; using the full knowledge and skills of each team member effectively; communicating one's own role clearly to patients, families, and team members.

3. **Interprofessional Communication**: Choosing effective communication tools and techniques; communicating information clearly and concisely in a form that supports the team's work; listening actively; recognizing how one's own personal style, culture, and professional background influence communication.

4. **Teams and Teamwork**: Applying team-based principles to planning and delivering patient care; engaging in shared decision-making; sharing accountability for outcomes; supporting each other during stressful or complex situations.

**TeamSTEPPS** (Team Strategies and Tools to Enhance Performance and Patient Safety) is an evidence-based training program developed by the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) and the U.S. Department of Defense that operationalizes IPEC competencies in clinical settings (King et al., 2008). TeamSTEPPS provides specific communication tools and strategies — SBAR, callout, check-back, handoffs, CUS — that help healthcare teams communicate more effectively, prevent errors, and manage high-stress situations.

Key TeamSTEPPS communication tools include:

| Tool | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **SBAR** | Structured handoff/escalation format (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) | Communicating a patient's change in status to the physician |
| **Callout** | Sharing important information with all team members simultaneously during an emergency | "Patient's BP is 80/40 — calling the rapid response team now" |
| **Check-back** | Closed-loop communication to confirm that a message was received and understood correctly | Receiver repeats the verbal order back to the sender before implementing |
| **CUS** | Assertive statement framework: "I am Concerned, I am Uncomfortable, this is a Safety issue" | Used when a team member needs to escalate a concern assertively |
| **I-PASS** | Structured handoff tool (Illness severity, Patient summary, Action list, Situation awareness, Synthesis by receiver) | Used during shift change or transfer handoffs |

> **Nursing Safety Priority: Speaking Up for Patient Safety**
> Research consistently shows that failures in communication are among the leading contributors to preventable adverse events in hospitals (The Joint Commission, 2021). Medical-surgical nurses must cultivate the professional courage to speak up when they observe a safety concern, regardless of the seniority or professional status of the colleague involved. Institutional cultures that support psychological safety — in which team members feel safe raising concerns without fear of retribution — are associated with lower rates of medication errors, falls, and patient harm.

## Section 1.5 — The Current Medical-Surgical Nursing Landscape

### 1.5.1 Trends in Adult Inpatient Care

The landscape of medical-surgical nursing practice is shaped by powerful demographic, epidemiologic, financial, and technological forces that every nurse entering the specialty must understand.

**An aging population.** The U.S. Census Bureau projects that by 2030, all baby boomers will be older than 65, and the population over 65 will exceed 73 million — nearly one-fifth of the total U.S. population (U.S. Census Bureau, 2018). Older adults are hospitalized at rates significantly higher than younger adults, and their hospitalizations are more complex, more prolonged, and more likely to involve multiple comorbidities, polypharmacy, cognitive impairment, and functional decline. Medical-surgical nurses must be competent in geriatric care principles: fall prevention, delirium recognition and management, pressure injury prevention, appropriate analgesic selection in older adults, and transitions of care coordination for patients returning to community settings.

**Increasing patient acuity.** Decades of compressed hospital length of stay and the growth of ambulatory care have concentrated the sickest patients in inpatient settings. Patients who would historically have been admitted to ICUs are increasingly managed on medical-surgical floors equipped with telemetry and closer monitoring. Patients are being discharged earlier in their recovery, meaning that those who remain in the hospital at any given time are, on average, at an earlier and more precarious stage of their recovery trajectory. This compression of complexity into the acute setting demands that medical-surgical nurses maintain a level of clinical vigilance that was previously associated primarily with critical care.

**Chronic disease burden.** The majority of hospitalized adults in the United States have at least one chronic disease, and many have multiple comorbidities. Heart disease, diabetes, obesity, COPD, chronic kidney disease, and hypertension are among the most prevalent diagnoses contributing to hospital admissions. Managing patients with multiple concurrent chronic conditions requires not only technical knowledge of each condition but sophisticated judgment about how conditions and their treatments interact — for example, how aggressive diuresis for heart failure must be balanced against the risk of acute kidney injury in a patient with pre-existing chronic kidney disease.

**Behavioral health integration.** Substance use disorders, depression, anxiety, and serious mental illness are highly prevalent among medically hospitalized adults. Studies consistently show that patients with comorbid psychiatric conditions have longer hospital stays, more complications, and worse outcomes across a broad range of medical conditions (Druss & Walker, 2011). Medical-surgical nurses increasingly care for patients undergoing alcohol withdrawal, patients with opioid use disorder, patients in psychiatric crises, and patients with complex trauma histories. Competence in trauma-informed care, de-escalation, and behavioral health assessment is now a core expectation for the specialty.

### 1.5.2 Staffing, Acuity, and Technology

**Nurse staffing** is the single most extensively studied variable in the medical-surgical nursing literature, and the evidence linking staffing ratios to patient outcomes is among the strongest in all of health services research. Landmark studies by Needleman et al. (2011) and Aiken et al. (2014) demonstrated dose-response relationships between nursing hours per patient day and rates of hospital mortality, failure to rescue, pressure injuries, falls, and healthcare-associated infections. In states without mandated ratios, many medical-surgical nurses routinely care for six, seven, or eight patients simultaneously — workloads that evidence suggests exceed safe thresholds.

California remains the only U.S. state with legislatively mandated nurse-to-patient ratios for medical-surgical units (1:5 during day and evening shifts under California law, AB 394). Several other states have implemented disclosure requirements or are in various stages of legislative debate. The advocacy of professional nursing organizations — the ANA, AMSN, and state nurses associations — has been central to advancing staffing safety legislation, and nurses at every career stage have a professional stake in these policy debates.

**Health information technology (HIT)** has transformed the clinical environment of the medical-surgical nurse. Electronic health record (EHR) systems — now present in the vast majority of U.S. hospitals — have replaced paper charts, standardized documentation formats, enabled computerized physician order entry (CPOE), and provided clinical decision support alerts. Bar-code medication administration (BCMA) systems have reduced medication errors at the point of administration. Smart infusion pumps with drug libraries reduce the risk of programming errors in IV therapy. Remote telemetry systems allow centralized monitoring of cardiac rhythms across multiple patients and units simultaneously.

However, technology also introduces new challenges. **Alarm fatigue** — the desensitization of nurses to monitor alarms resulting from high volumes of non-actionable alerts — is a recognized patient safety threat associated with adverse events including cardiac arrests (Drew et al., 2014). **Documentation burden** in EHR systems can consume hours of each shift, reducing the time available for direct patient care. **Interoperability** gaps between systems can interrupt the flow of clinical information at critical transition points. Medical-surgical nurses must be both technically competent in the use of HIT tools and critically aware of their limitations.

## Section 1.6 — Professional Organizations, Certification, and Career Development

### 1.6.1 AMSN and MSNCB

The **Academy of Medical-Surgical Nurses (AMSN)** is the professional membership organization representing medical-surgical nurses in the United States. Founded in 1991, AMSN provides members with access to continuing education, clinical practice resources, the peer-reviewed journal *MEDSURG Nursing*, national and regional conferences, mentorship programs, and advocacy resources. AMSN's publication of the *Medical-Surgical Nursing: Scope and Standards of Practice* establishes the specialty's professional standards and serves as the foundational reference for CMSRN certification content.

The **Medical-Surgical Nursing Certification Board (MSNCB)** is the independent credentialing organization that administers specialty certification examinations for medical-surgical nurses. The MSNCB is separate from AMSN but works in close partnership with it. The MSNCB administers two primary certification examinations: the Certified Medical-Surgical Registered Nurse (CMSRN) examination and the Progressive Care Certified Nurse (PCCN) examination (the latter administered in partnership with the American Association of Critical-Care Nurses).

### 1.6.2 CMSRN and PCCN Certification

**CMSRN (Certified Medical-Surgical Registered Nurse)** is the specialty certification credential for registered nurses practicing in medical-surgical settings. Eligibility requirements include:

- A current, active, unencumbered RN license
- A minimum of 2 years of experience as a registered nurse
- At least 2,000 hours of clinical practice in medical-surgical nursing within the most recent 3 years, with at least 1,000 hours within the past year

The CMSRN examination tests knowledge across the full scope of medical-surgical nursing practice, including cardiovascular, respiratory, gastrointestinal, genitourinary, endocrine, neurological, oncological, musculoskeletal, and integumentary system conditions, as well as psychosocial, pharmacological, professional, and perioperative nursing content.

Research consistently demonstrates that nurses who achieve specialty certification report higher levels of professional satisfaction, greater confidence in their clinical judgment, and stronger commitment to their employing institution (Niebuhr & Biel, 2007). Patients cared for by certified nurses on units with higher proportions of certified staff have lower rates of failure to rescue and adverse events. Many hospital systems incentivize certification through salary differentials, annual bonuses, or recognition programs.

**PCCN (Progressive Care Certified Nurse)** is a certification for nurses who care for acutely ill patients who do not require intensive care but have complex needs that exceed routine medical-surgical care — a patient population often described as "step-down" or "intermediate care." The PCCN examination emphasizes cardiovascular, pulmonary, and neurological conditions in the acute care setting.

> **Clinical Alert: Specialty Certification and Professional Identity**
> Pursuing specialty certification is one of the most powerful investments a medical-surgical nurse can make in their professional development. Certification signals to patients, families, and colleagues that the nurse has demonstrated mastery of the specialty knowledge base and is committed to ongoing professional growth. If you are new to medical-surgical nursing, begin tracking your clinical hours now — 2,000 hours of medical-surgical practice is the threshold for CMSRN eligibility, and keeping a record from the start of your career will make the certification application process straightforward.

**Career development pathways** in medical-surgical nursing are diverse. Entry-level positions on general medical-surgical floors provide the broad clinical foundation from which nurses may specialize in telemetry, oncology, orthopedics, bariatrics, or other subspecialties. With experience, nurses may advance to charge nurse, preceptor, clinical educator, or unit manager roles. Graduate education opens pathways to clinical nurse specialist (CNS), nurse practitioner (NP), nurse educator, or nursing administrator positions. Many medical-surgical nurses find that the breadth of their specialty experience is uniquely valuable preparation for advanced practice roles, where comprehensive patient assessment and care coordination skills are central.

> **Professional Tip: Building Your Professional Portfolio from Day One**
> Begin documenting your professional accomplishments, continuing education hours, committee memberships, quality improvement projects, and clinical experiences from your first day on the unit. A professional portfolio — whether maintained digitally or in a binder — provides the evidence you need for certification applications, performance reviews, and career advancement opportunities. Many nurses are surprised to discover how much professional activity they have accumulated when they finally sit down to compile it.

## Chapter Summary

1. **Medical-surgical nursing** is the largest specialty in nursing, defined by the care of adult patients with acute and chronic medical conditions and those recovering from surgery, across a wide range of inpatient and ambulatory settings.

2. **The scope of medical-surgical nursing** encompasses virtually all adult medical and surgical diagnoses, requiring nurses to maintain broad and current knowledge across multiple body systems and disease categories.

3. **The specialty's historical roots** lie in military nursing (Crimean War, American Civil War, World War II), and its development has been driven by advances in medical technology, hospital financing changes, and the evidence-based practice movement.

4. **Core nursing roles** — caregiver, educator, advocate, care coordinator, and leader — are performed simultaneously by the medical-surgical nurse throughout every shift, requiring professional knowledge, communication skill, and ethical commitment.

5. **Interprofessional collaborative practice** is the standard of care in contemporary medical-surgical settings; effective team communication using structured tools (SBAR, TeamSTEPPS, I-PASS) is a patient safety imperative.

6. **Current trends** shaping medical-surgical nursing include the aging of the population, increasing patient acuity, growing chronic disease burden, integration of behavioral health, nurse staffing challenges, and the transformative impact of health information technology.

7. **The AMSN and MSNCB** provide the professional infrastructure for medical-surgical nursing: scope and standards documents, continuing education, peer-reviewed literature, and specialty certification (CMSRN, PCCN).

8. **Specialty certification** is associated with higher professional satisfaction, stronger clinical confidence, lower patient adverse event rates, and greater institutional commitment — making it one of the most impactful career investments a medical-surgical nurse can make.

## NCLEX-Style Review Questions

**1.** A nurse is caring for four patients on a medical-surgical unit. Which patient requires the nurse's immediate attention?

A. A 58-year-old with heart failure who is awaiting dietary consultation  
B. A 71-year-old post-appendectomy patient who requests pain medication (current pain rating 5/10)  
C. A 65-year-old with COPD whose oxygen saturation has decreased from 94% to 88% in the last 30 minutes  
D. A 79-year-old with a UTI who is asking for assistance to the bathroom

**Correct Answer: C**
*Rationale: A declining oxygen saturation — especially a drop of 6 percentage points in 30 minutes — signals potentially life-threatening respiratory deterioration and requires immediate assessment and intervention. The other patients have needs that are important but not immediately life-threatening.*

**2.** The nurse is preparing to delegate the measurement of vital signs to a nursing assistant. Which of the following represents appropriate application of the five rights of delegation? Select all that apply.

A. Confirming that vital sign measurement is within the nursing assistant's scope of practice  
B. Ensuring the nursing assistant understands how to report any abnormal findings  
C. Transferring accountability for the patient's outcomes to the nursing assistant  
D. Providing clear instructions about which patients and vital signs to obtain  
E. Verifying that the nursing assistant has the skills and competency to perform the task

**Correct Answers: A, B, D, E**
*Rationale: The five rights of delegation are right task, right circumstance, right person, right direction and communication, and right supervision. The RN always retains accountability for patient outcomes even when tasks are delegated; accountability cannot be transferred to a nursing assistant (eliminating option C).*

**3.** A medical-surgical nurse is using the SBAR communication format to report a patient's change in condition to the physician. Which statement is an example of the "Recommendation" component of SBAR?

A. "The patient is a 68-year-old man admitted two days ago for community-acquired pneumonia."  
B. "His temperature has risen from 37.8°C this morning to 39.4°C in the last two hours, and his white blood cell count is pending."  
C. "I believe his pneumonia may be worsening and that he requires a repeat chest X-ray and possible adjustment of his antibiotic therapy."  
D. "I am calling about Mr. Jones in Room 412, who has developed a high fever."

**Correct Answer: C**
*Rationale: The Recommendation component of SBAR is the nurse's clear statement of what action is needed. Option A represents Background, option B represents Assessment findings, and option D represents the Situation (the reason for the call).*

**4.** A nurse is evaluating outcomes for a patient who was admitted with a hip fracture and underwent open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF). Which finding best indicates a positive response to nursing interventions related to postoperative mobility?

A. The patient rates pain as 4/10 with current analgesic regimen  
B. The patient ambulates 50 feet with a walker and supervision, with stable gait  
C. The patient's surgical wound is healing without signs of infection  
D. The patient verbalizes understanding of weight-bearing restrictions

**Correct Answer: B**
*Rationale: Actual demonstration of ambulation is the most direct behavioral indicator of successful mobility-focused interventions. The other options reflect important but different outcome domains: pain control (A), wound integrity (C), and patient education (D).*

**5.** The medical-surgical unit on which a nurse works has recently experienced several near-miss events involving medication errors at the point of administration. Which interprofessional approach is most likely to be effective in reducing these events?

A. Requiring physicians to write all medication orders by hand rather than using CPOE  
B. Implementing a bar-code medication administration (BCMA) system with standardized pharmacist review at point of dispensing  
C. Reducing the number of medications administered per shift by simplifying patient medication regimens  
D. Assigning a nurse supervisor to observe every medication administration on the unit

**Correct Answer: B**
*Rationale: Bar-code medication administration (BCMA) is an evidence-based technology intervention that has demonstrated significant reductions in medication errors at the point of administration. When combined with pharmacist review (an interprofessional element), BCMA addresses multiple error pathways. Option A would likely increase transcription errors. Options C and D are not practical or evidence-based system solutions.*

**6.** Which of the following statements accurately describes the role of the registered nurse in interprofessional collaborative practice on a medical-surgical unit?

A. The nurse's primary role is to implement physician orders efficiently and report results accurately.  
B. The nurse coordinates and integrates care from multiple disciplines, functioning as the central communication hub due to continuous bedside presence.  
C. The nurse defers clinical assessment to the physician and focuses exclusively on direct patient care tasks.  
D. The nurse communicates primarily with patients and families and avoids initiating contact with other disciplines to maintain professional boundaries.

**Correct Answer: B**
*Rationale: The registered nurse is uniquely positioned as the continuous presence at the bedside, enabling a coordinating and integrating role within the interprofessional team. Nursing practice encompasses assessment, judgment, advocacy, and interprofessional communication — not merely task implementation (eliminating A) or passive deference (eliminating C and D).*

**7.** A new graduate nurse is establishing professional development goals. Which of the following activities would best support achieving CMSRN certification in three years?

A. Enrolling immediately in an advanced practice nursing program  
B. Tracking clinical hours in medical-surgical nursing from the start of employment, pursuing relevant CE, and reviewing the AMSN scope and standards  
C. Requesting transfer to an ICU position after six months of medical-surgical experience  
D. Focusing exclusively on building technical skills for the first two years before beginning any formal professional development activities

**Correct Answer: B**
*Rationale: CMSRN eligibility requires a minimum of 2,000 hours of medical-surgical clinical practice, with at least 1,000 hours in the most recent year. Beginning to track hours immediately, engaging with professional resources, and pursuing continuing education positions the nurse to meet eligibility requirements and succeed on the examination. Option A addresses a different credential. Options C and D would either change the focus of the nurse's experience or delay professional development.*

**8.** A nurse recognizes that a patient is experiencing alcohol withdrawal seizures. Which action reflects the nurse's role as patient advocate?

A. Waiting for the physician to independently recognize and address the issue  
B. Notifying the charge nurse and physician immediately, stating clearly that the patient requires urgent evaluation and management  
C. Administering a benzodiazepine from an emergency medication supply without a physician order  
D. Documenting the seizure in the electronic health record and continuing scheduled nursing tasks

**Correct Answer: B**
*Rationale: Patient advocacy requires the nurse to act assertively on the patient's behalf by immediately communicating the clinical urgency to appropriate team members. Option A is passive and endangers the patient. Option C violates medication administration standards. Option D fails to prioritize the patient's urgent need.*

**9.** The charge nurse is assigning patients at the beginning of the shift. Which factor is most important to consider when determining safe nurse-to-patient assignments on a medical-surgical unit?

A. The nurses' preferred patient populations based on personal interest  
B. Nursing experience levels and the acuity and complexity of individual patients  
C. Minimizing the number of admissions and discharges during the shift  
D. Balancing the geographical location of patient rooms to minimize walking distance

**Correct Answer: B**
*Rationale: Safe staffing assignments require matching nurse competence and experience levels to patient acuity and complexity. Room geography (D) and personal preference (A) are secondary considerations. While managing workflow (C) is important, it is not the primary determinant of safe assignment.*

**10.** A medical-surgical nurse is caring for a 72-year-old patient admitted for a hip replacement who develops new-onset confusion on postoperative day 2. Which nursing action is the highest priority?

A. Applying bilateral soft wrist restraints to prevent the patient from pulling out IV lines  
B. Performing a systematic assessment for reversible causes of delirium and notifying the physician  
C. Requesting a psychiatric consultation for the patient's new behavioral changes  
D. Reassuring the family that some confusion is normal after surgery in older adults and requires no intervention

**Correct Answer: B**
*Rationale: Postoperative delirium in older adults is a medical emergency associated with increased mortality, prolonged hospitalization, and long-term cognitive decline. The nurse's first priority is a systematic assessment to identify and address potentially reversible causes (pain, urinary retention, hypoxia, medication effects, infection) and immediate notification of the physician. Restraints (A) increase delirium severity. Psychiatric consultation (C) may be appropriate but is not the first action. Reassurance without assessment (D) is unsafe.*

## Unfolding Case Study: Meeting Margaret

**Scene 1:**

Margaret Chen, 68 years old, arrives on the medical-surgical unit from the emergency department at 1430. She has been admitted for evaluation of worsening shortness of breath and bilateral leg swelling over the past five days. Her emergency department workup revealed a BNP of 1,840 pg/mL, mild hyponatremia (Na 131 mEq/L), and bilateral pleural effusions on chest X-ray. She has a history of heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), type 2 diabetes mellitus, and stage 3 chronic kidney disease. Her current medications include furosemide 40 mg oral daily, carvedilol 12.5 mg twice daily, lisinopril 5 mg daily, and metformin 1,000 mg twice daily. On arrival, her vital signs are: BP 158/94 mmHg, HR 96 bpm (irregular), RR 22 breaths/min, SpO₂ 91% on room air, temperature 37.2°C. She is sitting upright and appears uncomfortable, with audible wheezing on exertion. Her legs are edematous from ankles to knees, 3+ bilaterally.

**Scene 1 Questions:**

*Q1: What are the nurse's three highest-priority assessments in the first 15 minutes of receiving Margaret?*

**Answer:** (1) Respiratory assessment — auscultate breath sounds bilaterally; assess work of breathing, use of accessory muscles, and respiratory rate; apply supplemental oxygen immediately to raise SpO₂ to ≥94%; (2) Cardiovascular assessment — assess heart rate and rhythm, blood pressure, presence of S3 gallop or jugular venous distension; (3) Neurological/mental status assessment — determine whether Margaret is oriented and interactive, as mental status change may indicate cerebral hypoperfusion. Simultaneously, establish IV access, begin cardiac monitoring, and review all orders.

*Q2: The physician orders IV furosemide 80 mg now and metformin is to be held. Why would the nurse anticipate holding metformin in this clinical scenario?*

**Answer:** Metformin is contraindicated or requires caution in patients with acute kidney injury, significant hemodynamic instability, or those receiving IV contrast dye (which Margaret may receive for imaging). In decompensated heart failure with stage 3 CKD, diuresis can transiently worsen kidney function, increasing the risk of lactic acidosis — a rare but life-threatening complication of metformin. Holding metformin during the acute admission and until kidney function is stable and reassessed is consistent with evidence-based practice and the FDA's guidance on metformin use in renal impairment.

---

**Scene 2:**

Four hours later, Margaret has received IV furosemide and has produced 1,200 mL of urine. Her SpO₂ has improved to 94% on 4 L/min nasal cannula, and she reports feeling "a little better." Her repeat vital signs: BP 142/88, HR 88 bpm (irregular), RR 18 breaths/min. However, a repeat serum potassium level returns at 3.0 mEq/L (reference range 3.5–5.0 mEq/L). Margaret is on continuous cardiac monitoring. The nurse notes frequent premature ventricular complexes (PVCs) on the monitor.

*Q3: How should the nurse interpret the relationship between Margaret's potassium level and her cardiac monitoring findings? What is the priority nursing action?*

**Answer:** Hypokalemia (K 3.0 mEq/L) is a known consequence of loop diuretic therapy and increases myocardial excitability, which can manifest as PVCs, supraventricular tachycardia, or in severe cases, ventricular fibrillation. The nurse should notify the physician immediately, reporting the potassium level and the presence of PVCs on telemetry. The nurse should anticipate an order for oral or intravenous potassium replacement and continue to monitor the cardiac rhythm closely. Potassium replacement must be administered carefully — too rapid IV replacement can cause fatal hyperkalemia; institutional protocols for IV potassium administration (concentration, rate, route) must be followed precisely.

---

**Scene 3:**

On postoperative day 2 of Margaret's hospitalization, she is preparing for discharge. Her weight has decreased by 4.2 kg (approximately 9 lb) from admission, indicating effective diuresis. Her SpO₂ is 95% on room air, and she is ambulating with a walker in the hallway. Her discharge medications have been reconciled, and the case manager has arranged a home health nursing visit in 48 hours. The physician has ordered patient education prior to discharge on: low-sodium diet (2,000 mg/day), daily weight monitoring, medication compliance, symptom recognition, and when to call or return to the emergency department.

*Q4: Using the teach-back method, how should the nurse prioritize and evaluate Margaret's understanding of her discharge instructions, given her multiple comorbidities and the volume of information to convey?*

**Answer:** The nurse should prioritize the most critical, potentially life-saving information first: (1) the "danger signs" that require immediate action — weight gain of more than 2 lb in one day or 5 lb in one week, new or worsening shortness of breath, increased leg swelling, or feeling faint or dizzy — and when to call vs. when to go to the emergency department. (2) Next, teach daily weight monitoring with a specific weight at which to call the physician. (3) Then teach the name and purpose of each medication, particularly changes from prior regimen. (4) Teach-back should be used for each key concept: "I want to make sure I explained this clearly — can you tell me in your own words what you should do if you gain more than two pounds in one day?" The nurse should assess for barriers (vision, hearing, health literacy, stress) and involve Margaret's family or support person with her permission. Written materials in appropriate language and reading level should supplement verbal teaching, and the home health nurse should be notified of which areas to reinforce.
