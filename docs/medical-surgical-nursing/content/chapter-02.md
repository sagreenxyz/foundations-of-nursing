## Introduction

On a 28-bed medical-surgical unit at a regional medical center, a nurse is mid-shift when three patient situations compete for attention simultaneously. In Room 216, an 82-year-old woman with a hip fracture repair reports new-onset chest pain radiating to her jaw. In Room 220, a 58-year-old man with diabetes and a leg wound has a blood glucose of 38 mg/dL, and his family is alarmed at his confusion. Down the hall, in Room 224, a 71-year-old post-cholecystectomy patient is due for an ambulation session that the physical therapist has just requested — and the patient's call light has been lit for twelve minutes.

Three patients. Three problems. One nurse. Thirty seconds to decide who comes first, what information to gather, which action to take, and what to communicate — and to whom.

This scenario is not exceptional. It is the daily reality of medical-surgical nursing. The ability to navigate this reality safely, effectively, and compassionately is called **clinical judgment** — and it is the most essential cognitive skill a medical-surgical nurse can possess.

Clinical judgment is not the same as instinct, and it is not the same as following a checklist. It is a systematic, evidence-grounded, patient-centered process of gathering information, interpreting cues, setting priorities, making decisions, acting, and evaluating outcomes — then repeating the cycle. It is structured by two complementary frameworks: the **nursing process** (the operational scaffold of nursing practice) and the **NCSBN Clinical Judgment Measurement Model** (the cognitive architecture underlying competent nursing decisions).

This chapter explores both frameworks in depth and applies them to the medical-surgical setting. Section 2.1 defines clinical judgment and distinguishes it from related concepts. Section 2.2 presents the NCSBN Clinical Judgment Measurement Model and its six cognitive skills. Section 2.3 examines each phase of the nursing process (ADPIE) in medical-surgical practice. Section 2.4 addresses nursing diagnosis, patient outcomes, and care planning. Section 2.5 explores priority-setting frameworks and clinical reasoning models. Section 2.6 addresses documentation, SBAR communication, and the legal and ethical dimensions of clinical practice. Throughout, a recurring case study follows a patient through each framework to illustrate their integration in real clinical conditions.

## Section 2.1: Clinical Judgment — Definition and Significance

### 2.1.1 Defining Clinical Judgment

Clinical judgment is defined by the National Council of State Boards of Nursing (2023) as "the observed outcome of critical thinking and decision-making" — the observable, evaluable product of a nurse's cognitive engagement with a patient situation. It encompasses the full cognitive and behavioral arc from perceiving a cue to evaluating whether an intervention achieved its intended outcome.

Clinical judgment is distinct from, though related to, several adjacent concepts. **Critical thinking** is the broad intellectual discipline of rigorous analysis and evaluation that undergirds clinical judgment; it is the foundational skill set rather than the nursing-specific application. **Clinical reasoning** is the active, situation-specific process of gathering and interpreting patient data, generating hypotheses, and selecting actions — it is the process, while clinical judgment is the output. **Decision-making** refers to the selection of a course of action from among alternatives; it is one phase within clinical reasoning rather than a synonym for the whole (Tanner, 2006).

In medical-surgical nursing, clinical judgment operates under conditions of significant complexity and constraint. Nurses simultaneously manage multiple patients with different diagnoses, acuity levels, and care needs; they work within institutional protocols and physician order sets; they function as frontline sensors for patient deterioration; and they are legally and ethically accountable for the quality and safety of their decisions. The consequences of poor clinical judgment are concrete: missed deterioration, delayed intervention, preventable harm, and — in extreme cases — patient death.

> **Professional Tip: Developing Clinical Judgment**
> Clinical judgment is developed through structured reflection, not simply accumulated time. After each patient interaction, ask: What cues did I notice? What did I hypothesize? Was I right? What would I do differently? This reflective habit, sometimes called "thinking about thinking," accelerates professional growth and is associated with improved patient outcomes (Levett-Jones et al., 2010).

### 2.1.2 Why Clinical Judgment Matters in Medical-Surgical Practice

Several characteristics of the medical-surgical environment make strong clinical judgment especially consequential:

**Patient acuity and complexity.** Medical-surgical patients are not simply "stable" patients awaiting discharge. They frequently carry multiple comorbidities, are actively undergoing physiologic transitions (post-surgical, post-procedure, medication titration), and may deteriorate rapidly with little warning. A nurse who cannot distinguish expected from unexpected findings — or who normalizes abnormal data — risks failing to rescue (Clarke & Aiken, 2003).

**High patient-to-nurse ratios.** The typical medical-surgical nurse is responsible for four to six patients simultaneously. This ratio compresses the time available for individual assessment and makes priority-setting — a cornerstone of clinical judgment — essential to safe care delivery.

**Interprofessional communication demands.** The medical-surgical nurse serves as the primary integrator of clinical information across the team. Clinical judgment determines what information is communicated, how urgently, and to whom — making it a lever for both quality care and preventable harm.

**The nexus of deterioration and rescue.** Studies by Aiken et al. (2014) and Needleman et al. (2011) established that nurse education level and staffing ratios are independently associated with in-hospital mortality. The mechanism linking these variables is clinical judgment: educated, present nurses are better positioned to detect early deterioration signals and act before rescue becomes impossible.

The NCSBN formalized the importance of clinical judgment in 2023 by replacing the traditional NCLEX framework with the Next Generation NCLEX (NGN), an examination grounded entirely in the assessment of clinical judgment using the NCJMM. For nursing students and practicing nurses alike, understanding the NCJMM is now inseparable from professional competency.

## Section 2.2: The NCSBN Clinical Judgment Measurement Model

### 2.2.1 Overview and Structure of the NCJMM

The NCSBN Clinical Judgment Measurement Model was developed over a decade of research into how nurses think, decide, and act (NCSBN, 2023). It describes clinical judgment as a set of six **cognitive skills**, applied iteratively in response to patient cues:

| Cognitive Skill | Description |
|-----------------|-------------|
| **Recognize Cues** | Identifying relevant patient data from the environment, medical record, physical assessment, and clinical context |
| **Analyze Cues** | Determining the significance of identified cues by linking them to pathophysiology, pharmacology, and patient history |
| **Prioritize Hypotheses** | Generating and ranking possible explanations for the patient's condition based on urgency, probability, and risk |
| **Generate Solutions** | Identifying evidence-based interventions aligned with the prioritized hypothesis and the patient's goals |
| **Take Action** | Implementing the selected intervention(s), including direct care, communication, and delegation |
| **Evaluate Outcomes** | Assessing whether interventions achieved expected outcomes and adjusting the plan accordingly |

The model is explicitly iterative: evaluation of outcomes may prompt re-recognition of new cues, launching another cycle. This reflects the reality of bedside care, where conditions evolve and the nurse's cognitive engagement never truly stops.

The NCJMM is also **contextual**: it acknowledges that clinical judgment does not occur in a vacuum but is shaped by the patient's environment, the nurse's knowledge and experience, and the culture of the healthcare setting. A nurse with ten years of medical-surgical experience will recognize subtler cues and generate more refined hypotheses than a novice — but the NCJMM provides a shared framework that novices can use to develop their reasoning and experienced nurses can use to structure education and mentorship.

> **Evidence-Based Practice: The Next Generation NCLEX and Clinical Judgment**
> Beginning in April 2023, the NCSBN replaced the traditional NCLEX with the Next Generation NCLEX (NGN), an examination specifically designed to assess the six cognitive skills of the NCJMM. NGN item types include enhanced hot spots, extended drag-and-drop, extended matrix questions, cloze items, and clinical judgment case studies. Research by the NCSBN (2023) demonstrated that NGN item types more accurately predict new-graduate nursing practice readiness than traditional multiple-choice items, particularly in identifying students at risk for clinical errors in complex situations.

### 2.2.2 Applying the NCJMM in Medical-Surgical Practice

To illustrate how the six cognitive skills operate in practice, consider the following scenario:

A 74-year-old man, Mr. Alvarez, is post-operative day 1 following a right total hip arthroplasty. The nurse enters his room for the 0800 assessment and notes the following: his respiratory rate is 24 breaths/min (baseline 16), he is using pursed-lip breathing, and his SpO₂ has dropped from 96% to 89% on room air. He reports feeling "short of breath and anxious." He is afebrile. His left calf appears slightly more swollen than his right.

- **Recognize Cues:** Tachypnea, SpO₂ drop, accessory breathing pattern, subjective dyspnea, unilateral calf swelling in a post-surgical patient.
- **Analyze Cues:** Post-operative patients are at elevated risk for deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE). Unilateral calf swelling, dyspnea, and desaturation constitute a classic PE presentation. Afebrile status reduces the probability of pneumonia as the primary diagnosis.
- **Prioritize Hypotheses:** Pulmonary embolism is the highest-priority hypothesis given the risk profile and cue cluster; pneumonia and atelectasis remain differential considerations but are ranked lower.
- **Generate Solutions:** Apply supplemental oxygen immediately; obtain vital signs; notify the physician with SBAR; anticipate orders for CT pulmonary angiography, anticoagulation, and continuous monitoring; elevate HOB; stay at bedside.
- **Take Action:** Apply 4 L/min O₂ via nasal cannula, reassess SpO₂, call the physician using SBAR, document the change and actions in the EHR, and activate the rapid-response team if the patient's condition deteriorates before physician arrival.
- **Evaluate Outcomes:** After supplemental oxygen, SpO₂ improves to 94%. Physician orders stat CT-PA. The nurse continues to monitor for further deterioration. CT-PA confirms bilateral pulmonary emboli; anticoagulation is initiated.

This scenario illustrates that the NCJMM is not a theoretical abstraction — it is the cognitive map by which clinical judgment is exercised at the bedside.

## Section 2.3: The Nursing Process (ADPIE) in Medical-Surgical Practice

### 2.3.1 The Five Phases of the Nursing Process

The nursing process is a systematic, cyclical, problem-solving approach to nursing care that has served as the foundation of professional nursing practice since its formal description in the 1960s (Orlando, 1961; Yura & Walsh, 1967). It consists of five interdependent phases, abbreviated as **ADPIE**:

**Assessment** is the systematic collection of subjective and objective data about the patient's health status. In medical-surgical nursing, assessment includes the health history, physical examination, review of diagnostic results, medication reconciliation, psychosocial and cultural data, and the patient's own account of symptoms and preferences. Assessment data are gathered continuously — not only on admission — because medical-surgical patients' conditions can change rapidly.

Assessment data are classified as **subjective** (reported by the patient, such as pain or nausea) or **objective** (measured or observed by the nurse, such as vital signs, lung sounds, or wound appearance). Both types are essential. A patient who rates her pain as 2/10 but grimaces with every movement and guards her abdomen provides an incongruence between subjective and objective data that requires the nurse to explore further, not simply document the self-reported score.

**Diagnosis** is the interpretation of assessment data to identify actual or potential health problems. Nursing diagnoses are distinct from medical diagnoses: a medical diagnosis identifies a pathological process (e.g., *heart failure*), while a nursing diagnosis identifies the patient's response to that process and the human dimensions amenable to nursing intervention (e.g., *Decreased Cardiac Output related to altered myocardial contractility as evidenced by ejection fraction of 28%, fatigue, and dyspnea on exertion*).

**Planning** involves the formulation of patient-centered, measurable goals (outcomes) and the selection of nursing interventions designed to achieve those goals. Planning in medical-surgical settings is both short-term (immediate shift priorities) and long-term (discharge outcomes). Family members and the interprofessional team are incorporated into planning whenever possible and appropriate.

**Implementation** is the execution of the nursing care plan — providing direct care, administering medications, teaching patients and families, coordinating with the interprofessional team, delegating appropriately, and documenting all actions. Implementation in medical-surgical nursing is guided by institutional policies, clinical protocols, and the nurse's professional judgment.

**Evaluation** is the assessment of whether patient outcomes have been achieved and whether the care plan should be continued, modified, or discontinued. Evaluation is not a one-time event at the end of care; it is continuous. Every patient interaction provides evaluation data. The findings of evaluation feed directly back into reassessment, making the nursing process genuinely cyclical rather than linear.

> **Clinical Alert: The Nursing Process Is Not Paperwork**
> Students and new graduates sometimes perceive the nursing process as a documentation formality — something done *after* care to satisfy charting requirements. This is a dangerous misperception. The nursing process is the *cognitive framework* that drives care. Charting is the written record of a process that should already have occurred mentally. When the nursing process is bypassed — when nurses act on habit, routine, or assumption rather than assessed data — patient harm is more likely (Alfaro-LeFevre, 2020).

### 2.3.2 Assessment in Depth: Systematic and Focused Approaches

Medical-surgical nurses use two primary assessment strategies depending on patient acuity and context:

**Systematic (head-to-toe) assessment** is a comprehensive, structured evaluation of all body systems conducted on admission, at the start of each shift, and following significant changes in patient condition. It follows a consistent sequence — neurological, respiratory, cardiovascular, abdominal/gastrointestinal, genitourinary, musculoskeletal, integumentary, and psychosocial — to ensure no system is omitted. The systematic assessment establishes the patient's baseline and provides the data from which nursing diagnoses are formulated.

**Focused assessment** is a targeted evaluation of a specific system or problem in response to a patient report, change in condition, or follow-up on a known issue. When a patient reports chest pain, the nurse performs a focused cardiovascular and respiratory assessment — not a head-to-toe evaluation — in order to respond efficiently to the immediate concern. Focused assessments are performed continuously throughout the shift.

Both approaches depend on the nurse's **interview skills** (therapeutic communication, open-ended questions, clarification, and validation) and **physical examination techniques** (inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation). In medical-surgical settings, auscultation of lung sounds, assessment of bowel sounds, palpation of abdominal quadrants, and examination of skin integrity and peripheral pulses are daily nursing competencies, not physician-exclusive skills.

**Gordon's Functional Health Patterns** is one widely used framework for organizing nursing assessment across eleven functional areas: health perception and health management; nutrition and metabolism; elimination; activity and exercise; sleep and rest; cognition and perception; self-perception and self-concept; role and relationships; sexuality and reproduction; coping and stress tolerance; and values and beliefs. This framework helps ensure that assessment addresses the patient holistically — not only as a set of physiologic processes, but as a complete person embedded in relationships, culture, and meaning.

## Section 2.4: Nursing Diagnosis, Outcomes, and Planning

### 2.4.1 NANDA-I Nursing Diagnoses and Collaborative Problems

**NANDA International (NANDA-I)** is the organization that develops, reviews, and maintains the standardized taxonomy of nursing diagnoses used globally. A NANDA-I nursing diagnosis is defined as "a clinical judgment about a human experience/response to health conditions/life processes, or vulnerability to that experience or response, by an individual, family, group, or community" (NANDA-I, 2021, p. 17).

NANDA-I nursing diagnoses are classified into three types:

- **Problem-focused (actual) diagnoses** describe a patient's current response to a health problem: *Impaired Gas Exchange*, *Acute Pain*, *Activity Intolerance*, *Deficient Knowledge*.
- **Risk diagnoses** describe the patient's vulnerability to developing a problem: *Risk for Infection*, *Risk for Falls*, *Risk for Pressure Injury*.
- **Health-promotion diagnoses** describe motivation and readiness to improve health: *Readiness for Enhanced Self-Care*, *Readiness for Enhanced Nutrition*.

Each problem-focused or risk diagnosis is stated using a three-part **PES format**: **P**roblem (the NANDA-I label), **E**tiology (the related or risk factors — the "related to" clause), and **S**igns and Symptoms (the defining characteristics — the "as evidenced by" clause). A correctly stated nursing diagnosis might read: *Impaired Skin Integrity related to prolonged immobility and impaired circulation as evidenced by a 2 × 1.5 cm Stage II pressure injury over the right heel*.

**Collaborative problems** are distinct from nursing diagnoses. They represent physiological complications that the nurse monitors and manages jointly with the physician — complications that exceed the nurse's independent scope of practice to treat. They are typically stated as *Potential Complication of [medical condition or treatment]: [specific complication]*. For example, *Potential Complication of Coronary Artery Bypass Graft: Dysrhythmia* or *Potential Complication of Total Hip Arthroplasty: Pulmonary Embolism*. The nurse's role with collaborative problems is surveillance, early detection, and timely communication — not independent treatment.

Differentiating nursing diagnoses from collaborative problems is not an academic exercise; it directly shapes the care plan. For nursing diagnoses, the nurse designs, implements, and evaluates independent nursing interventions. For collaborative problems, the nurse monitors, reports, and implements physician-ordered interventions within defined parameters (Carpenito, 2022).

### 2.4.2 SMART Outcomes, NOC, and Care Planning

Effective care planning begins with well-formulated patient outcomes. The **SMART** framework provides the standard for outcome quality:

- **Specific:** Clearly describes what the patient will do or achieve ("Patient will ambulate 50 feet in the hallway with walker assistance").
- **Measurable:** Includes a quantifiable indicator ("Pain rated ≤3/10 on the numeric rating scale").
- **Achievable:** Realistic for the patient's current condition and resources.
- **Relevant:** Directly related to the nursing diagnosis and patient priorities.
- **Time-bound:** Specifies when the outcome should be achieved ("...by discharge," "...within 24 hours," "...by 1400 today").

Outcomes are oriented toward the patient — they describe what the **patient** will do or demonstrate, not what the **nurse** will do. A statement such as "The nurse will assess pain every two hours" is an intervention, not an outcome.

The **Nursing Outcomes Classification (NOC)** system provides a standardized vocabulary of patient outcomes that nurses can influence through their practice, rated on a five-point Likert-type scale. The **Nursing Interventions Classification (NIC)** provides a parallel taxonomy of nursing interventions. Together, NOC and NIC create a common language for care planning that facilitates communication, documentation, and research across settings (Moorhead et al., 2018; Butcher et al., 2018).

The care plan — whether formal (written/EHR-based) or mental — synthesizes the nursing diagnoses, SMART outcomes, and selected interventions into a coherent guide for the shift and beyond. Effective care plans are **individualized** (reflecting the specific patient's data, not generic textbook content), **prioritized** (organized from most urgent to least urgent), and **interprofessional** (incorporating goals and inputs from the entire team).

> **Professional Tip: Individualizing the Care Plan**
> Novice nurses sometimes produce care plans that could apply to any patient with a given diagnosis. Expert nurses produce plans that could apply only to *this* patient, on *this* day, with *these* specific defining characteristics, goals, and barriers. The difference is the depth and specificity of the assessment data driving the plan. The more precise the assessment, the more individualized — and more effective — the care plan.

## Section 2.5: Priority-Setting Frameworks and Clinical Reasoning

### 2.5.1 Frameworks for Priority Setting

Priority setting is the process of determining the sequence in which nursing problems and interventions will be addressed. In a low-acuity environment with one patient and unlimited time, priority setting is trivial. In the real medical-surgical environment — multiple patients, competing demands, limited time — it is a patient safety imperative.

Several frameworks guide priority-setting in medical-surgical nursing:

**ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation):** The foundational triage principle from emergency and basic life support medicine. Any problem threatening airway patency, respiratory function, or circulatory integrity is addressed before all others. In medical-surgical nursing, the ABCs remain the anchor for acute deterioration: a patient in respiratory distress takes precedence over a patient with wound care due, regardless of which intervention was scheduled first.

**Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs:** Abraham Maslow's hierarchy organizes human needs into five levels — physiological, safety, love/belonging, esteem, and self-actualization — with physiological and safety needs at the base. In nursing, this framework supports the prioritization of survival needs (oxygenation, hydration, pain control) before psychosocial needs (patient education, social support), while recognizing that both categories are genuine nursing responsibilities.

**Safety priorities:** Regardless of acuity hierarchy, any situation posing an immediate risk to patient safety — fall risk, medication error potential, risk of aspiration, suicidal ideation, elopement risk — must be addressed before non-urgent care. The Joint Commission's National Patient Safety Goals provide a structured framework for institutional safety priorities, including medication reconciliation, identification errors, and infection prevention.

**Survival threats versus non-survival threats:** Prioritize any finding that is potentially life-threatening or irreversible before those that are uncomfortable but not immediately dangerous. A patient's newly reported crushing chest pain takes precedence over a patient's request for a pain medication refill for mild back discomfort.

**Unstable versus stable:** An unstable patient (vital signs trending abnormally, altered mental status, new-onset symptoms) always takes precedence over a stable patient whose condition is unchanged from the previous assessment.

In practice, nurses apply these frameworks simultaneously and contextually, not in rigid hierarchy. When all patients are stable and no safety emergencies exist, priority shifts to which interventions are most time-sensitive (medications due, scheduled assessments), which problems carry the greatest consequence if delayed, and which patients are most likely to deteriorate without intervention.

> **Nursing Safety Priority: Speaking Up**
> Hierarchies within healthcare teams — real and perceived — can inhibit nurses from escalating concerns about patient deterioration. The medical-surgical nurse is the primary safety net for the hospitalized patient. When a nurse observes a change in a patient's condition that suggests deterioration, the obligation to speak up — to a senior nurse, charge nurse, physician, or rapid-response team — supersedes interpersonal discomfort or deference to authority. Speak Up programs, CUS words (Concerned, Uncomfortable, Safety), and the Two-Challenge Rule (TeamSTEPPS) provide scripted tools for assertive communication in hierarchical environments.

### 2.5.2 Clinical Reasoning Models

Several formal models support the development of structured clinical reasoning:

**Tanner's Model of Clinical Judgment (2006)** describes clinical judgment as a process in which noticing (perceiving relevant cues), interpreting (making sense of data), responding (selecting and implementing action), and reflecting (evaluating the appropriateness of the response) are integrated with the nurse's background knowledge and relationship with the patient. Tanner's model emphasizes the role of narrative knowing — the nurse's understanding of the patient as a person — as a crucial complement to propositional (textbook) knowledge.

**The OPT Model (Outcome-Present State Test Model)** developed by Pesut and Herman (1999) frames clinical reasoning as a "spinning" process in which the nurse simultaneously holds the patient's **present state** (current problems and data) and the desired **outcome state** (goals) in mind, using clinical reasoning to identify and close the gap. The model highlights that expert nurses think "from the outcome backward" — they keep the desired result in view while selecting interventions, preventing the drift into intervention-focused thinking that can occur when the outcome is poorly defined.

**Socratic questioning** is a reflective tool — a set of self-directed questions that challenge assumptions and probe the depth of clinical reasoning. Applied to clinical practice, it might include: What do I know about this patient? What do I not know that I need to know? What is the most dangerous explanation for this finding? What would happen if I waited? What would an expert nurse notice here that I might be missing?

These models are complementary rather than competing. Together, they support nurses in moving from task-based care (doing what is ordered or what is routine) to reasoning-based care (doing what the patient's current condition requires, grounded in evidence and guided by outcomes).

## Section 2.6: Documentation, Communication, and Legal Dimensions

### 2.6.1 Documentation Standards in Medical-Surgical Nursing

Documentation in medical-surgical nursing is not merely a bureaucratic obligation; it is a clinical, legal, and ethical responsibility. The nursing record communicates the patient's condition and the nurse's clinical reasoning to all members of the healthcare team, across shifts and settings, and across time. It is the primary legal record of what was observed, decided, and done.

The **Electronic Health Record (EHR)** has become the universal platform for nursing documentation in acute care settings. EHR systems support nursing documentation through structured templates (admission assessments, fall-risk tools, skin integrity forms, medication administration records), free-text nursing notes, flowsheets for vital signs and intake/output, and clinical decision support alerts. The EHR also provides the foundation for quality metrics reporting and regulatory compliance.

Several documentation formats guide nursing narrative notes:

**SOAP (Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan):** Organizes documentation around the patient's subjective report, the nurse's objective findings, the clinical assessment or interpretation, and the plan for follow-up. SOAP is used in both nursing and medical documentation and promotes a complete, logical entry.

**SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation):** The standard communication tool for nurse-to-physician and handoff communication. SBAR structures verbal or written reports to ensure that all essential information is conveyed in a predictable, efficient sequence. An SBAR communication for a patient with suspected pulmonary embolism might be structured as:

- **Situation:** "I'm calling about Mr. Alvarez in Room 218, post-op day 1 from a right total hip. He's having acute respiratory distress with SpO₂ of 89% on room air and is reporting shortness of breath."
- **Background:** "He has no prior history of PE. He received enoxaparin last night. His left calf is slightly more swollen than his right."
- **Assessment:** "I'm concerned he may be developing a pulmonary embolism."
- **Recommendation:** "I'd like you to come evaluate him immediately, and I'd like to discuss initiating oxygen and ordering a CT-PA."

**Bedside shift report (BSR):** A handoff communication model in which the nurse conducts the shift-change report at the patient's bedside with the patient and (when appropriate) family present. BSR has been associated with improved patient engagement, reduced call-light use, and improved nursing accountability for the care plan (Jeffs et al., 2013).

**I-PASS (Illness severity, Patient summary, Action list, Situation awareness, Synthesis by receiver):** An evidence-based handoff protocol developed in pediatric hospital medicine and increasingly applied in adult medical-surgical settings, demonstrating significant reductions in medical errors and adverse events (Starmer et al., 2014).

Medical-surgical nurses must document findings **contemporaneously** — as close as possible to the time of observation or action. Retroactive charting (documenting hours after the fact) is vulnerable to inaccuracy, creates medico-legal risk, and violates the spirit of the live medical record. Nurses must also document **objectively**: observations are recorded as data, not interpretations ("Patient grimaced and held abdomen when repositioned," not "Patient appeared to be in severe pain").

### 2.6.2 Legal and Ethical Dimensions of Clinical Judgment

Clinical judgment operates within a legal and ethical framework that is inseparable from the cognitive process itself. The nurse who fails to recognize a deteriorating patient and acts on that recognition is not only making a clinical error — they may be committing an act of professional negligence.

**Negligence and the standard of care.** In nursing, negligence is defined as the failure to act as a reasonably prudent nurse would act under similar circumstances (Sullivan, 2020). The **standard of care** — derived from professional guidelines (such as ANA standards and AMSN specialty standards), institutional policies, and established evidence — defines what a reasonably competent nurse would do in a given situation. Clinical judgment errors that fall below the standard of care and result in patient harm constitute the basis of professional liability.

**Documentation as legal protection.** The nursing record is the primary evidence in any legal proceeding involving patient care. Thorough, accurate, and contemporaneous documentation demonstrates that the nurse assessed the patient, recognized relevant changes, communicated findings appropriately, implemented interventions, and evaluated outcomes. Absent documentation is not simply an administrative problem — in litigation, the principle "if it wasn't charted, it wasn't done" applies.

**Mandatory reporting.** Medical-surgical nurses are mandated reporters in several legally defined situations: suspected elder abuse or neglect, child abuse or neglect (when the patient or a visitor is a minor at risk), certain communicable diseases, and in some jurisdictions, domestic violence disclosures. Failure to report in these situations carries both legal consequences and ethical culpability.

**Informed consent and patient autonomy.** While the physician is responsible for obtaining informed consent for procedures and surgical interventions, the nurse plays a crucial role in the informed consent process: ensuring the patient has received information in understandable terms, that questions have been answered, and that the patient's decision reflects their own values and preferences rather than coercion. When a nurse has reason to believe that a patient's consent was not truly informed — or that the patient has changed their mind — this must be communicated to the physician immediately and documented.

**Clinical judgment and ethical decision-making.** Clinical judgment is inextricably linked to ethical practice. Nurses routinely encounter ethical tensions: a patient who refuses a recommended treatment; a family demanding aggressive intervention that a dying patient has previously declined; a physician order that the nurse believes is incorrect or unsafe. The American Nurses Association (ANA) Code of Ethics for Nurses provides the professional ethical framework guiding these situations, emphasizing patient autonomy, advocacy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice, and integrity (ANA, 2015).

When the nurse's clinical judgment conflicts with a physician's order — particularly when the order appears unsafe — the appropriate response follows a clear sequence: clarify with the physician (there may be a rationale the nurse is not aware of); if the order remains concerning, invoke the chain of command (charge nurse, nursing supervisor, medical director); document the concern and the communication; and if all else fails and the nurse believes carrying out the order would cause direct harm, exercise the professional right and obligation to refuse the order and document the rationale fully (ANA, 2015).

## Chapter Summary

This chapter has established the cognitive and operational foundations of nursing practice in medical-surgical settings. The following key takeaways summarize its essential concepts:

1. **Clinical judgment** is the observable product of critical thinking and clinical reasoning — the nurse's response to a patient situation based on interpreted data, prioritized hypotheses, and selected actions. It is the essential cognitive skill of professional nursing.

2. **The NCSBN Clinical Judgment Measurement Model (NCJMM)** describes six cognitive skills — Recognize Cues, Analyze Cues, Prioritize Hypotheses, Generate Solutions, Take Action, and Evaluate Outcomes — that together constitute the architecture of safe nursing decision-making, as assessed by the Next Generation NCLEX.

3. **The nursing process (ADPIE)** provides the operational scaffold of nursing care: Assessment (systematic data collection), Diagnosis (identifying patient responses), Planning (formulating SMART outcomes and selecting interventions), Implementation (executing the plan), and Evaluation (assessing outcomes and adjusting care).

4. **Nursing diagnoses** (NANDA-I) identify patient responses to health conditions that nurses are licensed to treat, stated in PES format. They differ from medical diagnoses (pathological processes) and collaborative problems (physiological complications monitored jointly with physicians).

5. **SMART outcomes** are Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound statements of expected patient behavior or condition, oriented toward the patient rather than the nurse.

6. **Priority-setting frameworks** — ABCs, Maslow's hierarchy, safety priorities, and unstable-versus-stable — guide the medical-surgical nurse in determining which patients and problems require attention first, a decision with direct consequences for patient safety.

7. **Documentation** is a clinical, legal, and ethical responsibility. SBAR, SOAP, bedside shift report, and I-PASS provide structured communication frameworks. Accurate, contemporaneous, and objective charting is both a standard of practice and legal protection.

8. **Legal and ethical dimensions** of clinical judgment include the standard of care, documentation as evidence, mandatory reporting obligations, the informed consent process, and the nurse's professional right and obligation to refuse an unsafe order.

## NCLEX-Style Review Questions

**1.** A nurse is caring for a 66-year-old woman, post-operative day 2 from an abdominal hysterectomy, who reports sudden-onset right calf pain and mild shortness of breath. Which action should the nurse take **first**?

- A. Reassure the patient that calf pain is normal after surgery
- B. Administer the next scheduled dose of analgesic
- C. Assess the patient's calf, vital signs, and respiratory status, and notify the physician immediately
- D. Apply a warm compress to the patient's calf

**Correct Answer: C**
*Rationale: The combination of unilateral calf pain and dyspnea in a post-operative patient is a classic presentation of deep vein thrombosis with possible pulmonary embolism, both life-threatening conditions. The nurse must assess and notify the physician immediately. Reassurance (A) dismisses a potentially critical symptom. Analgesic administration (B) is contraindicated until the cause of the symptom is identified. Warm compress application (D) is contraindicated when DVT is suspected, as it may dislodge a clot.*

The nurse notes that a post-surgical patient has a heart rate of 118 bpm, blood pressure of 88/52 mmHg, and increasing anxiety, and suspects hypovolemic shock. Which cognitive skill is the nurse applying when linking these findings to their most probable cause?

- A. Recognize Cues
- B. Analyze Cues
- C. Generate Solutions
- D. Take Action

**Correct Answer: B**
*Rationale: Analyze Cues involves interpreting the significance of identified cues by connecting them to pathophysiology and clinical context. The nurse is interpreting the vital signs and anxiety — already recognized as cues — and linking them to hypovolemic shock. Recognize Cues (A) is the initial identification of relevant data. Generate Solutions (C) involves planning interventions. Take Action (D) involves implementing those interventions.*

**3.** A nurse formulates the following nursing diagnosis: *Impaired Gas Exchange related to alveolar-capillary membrane changes secondary to pneumonia as evidenced by SpO₂ of 88%, RR of 28, and patient report of "feeling like I can't get enough air."* Which component of this statement represents the **defining characteristics (signs and symptoms)**?

- A. Impaired Gas Exchange
- B. Related to alveolar-capillary membrane changes secondary to pneumonia
- C. SpO₂ of 88%, RR of 28, and patient report of "feeling like I can't get enough air"
- D. Alveolar-capillary membrane changes

**Correct Answer: C**
*Rationale: In the PES format, the defining characteristics (signs and symptoms) constitute the "as evidenced by" component — the observable or reported data that confirm the problem. SpO₂, respiratory rate, and the patient's subjective report are the defining characteristics. The problem (P) is Impaired Gas Exchange. The etiology (E) is the alveolar-capillary membrane changes.*

**4.** A nurse is planning care for four medical-surgical patients. Which patient should the nurse assess **first**?

- A. A 55-year-old patient with stable chronic obstructive pulmonary disease who requests help repositioning
- B. A 70-year-old patient post-op day 1 from knee arthroplasty who reports pain of 6/10 and is due for scheduled analgesics
- C. A 48-year-old patient who is reporting new-onset chest pain and diaphoresis
- D. A 63-year-old patient awaiting discharge teaching about wound care

**Correct Answer: C**
*Rationale: New-onset chest pain and diaphoresis are potential indicators of acute coronary syndrome — an immediately life-threatening condition requiring urgent assessment and intervention. This patient represents a survival threat and takes priority over stable discomfort (B), a repositioning request (A), and discharge teaching (D). The ABCs and the principle of survival-threat-first govern this priority decision.*

**5.** The nurse is communicating a change in patient condition to the physician using the SBAR format. In which component of SBAR would the nurse include the patient's current vital signs and recent laboratory results?

- A. Situation
- B. Background
- C. Assessment
- D. Recommendation

**Correct Answer: B**
*Rationale: Background in the SBAR format encompasses relevant contextual information, including recent vital signs, laboratory results, current medications, and pertinent medical history. Situation (A) is a brief description of the current concern. Assessment (C) is the nurse's clinical interpretation or hypothesis. Recommendation (D) is the nurse's requested action.*

**6.** A nurse is evaluating whether a patient outcome has been met. The outcome stated: "Patient will ambulate 100 feet in the hallway with walker assistance by 1400 on post-operative day 2." The patient ambulated 80 feet with a walker at 1430. Which evaluation statement is **most accurate**?

- A. Outcome fully met — patient ambulated with walker assistance
- B. Outcome not met — patient did not ambulate the goal distance within the stated time
- C. Outcome partially met — patient ambulated with walker but did not achieve the distance or time goal
- D. Outcome cannot be evaluated because ambulation distance is not a valid nursing outcome

**Correct Answer: C**
*Rationale: The outcome has three components: distance (100 feet), assistance modality (walker), and time (by 1400, post-op day 2). The patient used a walker (met) but walked 80 feet (not 100) and completed the activity at 1430 (not by 1400). This represents partial achievement. A plan modification — extending the time frame or adjusting the distance goal — would be the appropriate next step.*

**7.** Which of the following is an example of a **collaborative problem** rather than a nursing diagnosis?

- A. Acute Pain related to surgical incision as evidenced by patient report of pain 7/10
- B. Risk for Infection related to disruption of skin integrity
- C. Potential Complication of Cardiac Catheterization: Dysrhythmia
- D. Deficient Knowledge related to newly prescribed anticoagulant therapy

**Correct Answer: C**
*Rationale: Collaborative problems represent physiological complications that the nurse monitors and manages jointly with the physician, using both physician-prescribed and nursing interventions. "Potential Complication of Cardiac Catheterization: Dysrhythmia" is a complication the nurse cannot independently treat but must surveil and report. The other options (A, B, D) are nursing diagnoses representing patient responses within nursing's independent scope of treatment.*

**8.** A nurse is performing a shift assessment on a patient admitted for congestive heart failure. The patient states, "I feel fine," but the nurse observes 3+ bilateral pitting edema, a respiratory rate of 24 breaths/min, and audible crackles bilaterally. How should the nurse interpret this data?

- A. Document the patient's self-report; subjective data is more reliable than objective findings
- B. Recognize an incongruence between subjective and objective data requiring further assessment and physician notification
- C. Reassess in one hour, as crackles may resolve spontaneously
- D. Defer assessment to the physician, as the findings are beyond the nurse's scope

**Correct Answer: B**
*Rationale: When subjective data ("I feel fine") conflicts with objective findings (edema, tachypnea, crackles), the nurse must recognize the incongruence and investigate further. The objective findings suggest fluid overload and decompensating heart failure. Defaulting to the patient's self-report (A) ignores critical objective data. Waiting one hour (C) delays potentially urgent intervention. Deferring to the physician without nursing assessment (D) abdicates the nurse's assessment responsibility.*

**9.** Which of the following outcomes is written correctly in SMART format?

- A. "Nurse will ensure patient receives respiratory therapy twice daily."
- B. "Patient's breathing will improve."
- C. "Patient will demonstrate correct use of incentive spirometer to achieve a volume of ≥1,000 mL by the end of this shift."
- D. "Patient should understand how to use the incentive spirometer before discharge."

**Correct Answer: C**
*Rationale: Option C is specific (correct use of incentive spirometer, ≥1,000 mL), measurable (volume achieved), achievable (reasonable for post-operative day 1), relevant (respiratory outcome), and time-bound (by end of shift). Option A describes a nursing intervention, not a patient outcome. Option B lacks specificity and measurability. Option D uses vague language ("should understand") and lacks a measurable indicator.*

**10.** Select **all** that apply. Which of the following are correct principles of nursing documentation in the electronic health record?

- A. Document assessments and interventions as close to the time of occurrence as possible
- B. Correct an error by drawing a single line through the entry and initialing it (paper record) or using the electronic amendment function (EHR)
- C. Use subjective language to convey the severity of a patient's condition (e.g., "patient appeared very uncomfortable")
- D. Document objective observations rather than interpretations (e.g., "patient grimaced and guarded abdomen with repositioning")
- E. Retroactive charting is acceptable if the nurse remembers to note the actual time of the event
- F. The nursing record is a legal document and must accurately reflect all assessments, communications, and interventions

**Correct Answers: A, B, D, F**
*Rationale: Contemporaneous documentation (A), proper correction procedures (B), objective language (D), and recognition of the legal nature of the record (F) are all correct documentation principles. Subjective language (C) is inappropriate — observable behaviors are documented, not the nurse's emotional interpretation. Retroactive charting (E) creates legal and accuracy problems and is not a recognized best practice.*

## Unfolding Case Study: Caring for David

**Scene 1:**

David Reyes, 61 years old, is admitted to the medical-surgical unit from the emergency department with a chief complaint of severe abdominal pain (8/10), nausea, and vomiting for the past 18 hours. His vital signs on admission: BP 104/68 mmHg, HR 112 bpm, RR 20 breaths/min, temperature 38.7°C, SpO₂ 97% on room air. His abdomen is rigid and diffusely tender to palpation. Laboratory results reveal WBC 18,400/mm³, serum creatinine 1.9 mg/dL (baseline 1.1), and lipase 2,840 U/L (reference range 0–160 U/L). He has a history of alcohol use disorder, hypertension, and hyperlipidemia. He is diaphoretic and appears acutely ill. He is started on IV normal saline at 150 mL/hr, IV morphine PRN, nothing-by-mouth status, and IV ondansetron.

**Scene 1 Questions:**

*Q1: Using the NCJMM, what are the most critical cues the nurse should recognize in David's initial presentation, and what priority hypotheses should be generated?*

**Answer:** Critical cues include: fever (38.7°C), tachycardia (112 bpm), hypotension (104/68 mmHg), rigid and diffusely tender abdomen, markedly elevated lipase (2,840 U/L), leukocytosis (WBC 18,400), acute kidney injury (creatinine 1.9 from baseline 1.1), and diaphoresis. These cues collectively suggest **acute pancreatitis** as the primary diagnosis (dramatically elevated lipase, abdominal pain, history of alcohol use). However, the systemic inflammatory response (fever, tachycardia, hypotension) and rigid abdomen raise the concern for **severe acute pancreatitis with systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS)**, which carries significant morbidity and mortality risk. The nurse must also monitor for early **sepsis** (temperature + tachycardia + hypotension) and **acute kidney injury** (creatinine elevation). The highest-priority hypothesis is severe acute pancreatitis with evolving SIRS/sepsis, requiring aggressive fluid resuscitation and close monitoring.

*Q2: Formulate two NANDA-I nursing diagnoses for David using the PES format, and identify one collaborative problem.*

**Answer:**
- **Nursing Diagnosis 1:** *Acute Pain related to pancreatic inflammation and peritoneal irritation as evidenced by patient report of pain 8/10, rigid abdomen, diaphoresis, and tachycardia.*
- **Nursing Diagnosis 2:** *Deficient Fluid Volume related to vomiting, third-spacing, and NPO status as evidenced by BP 104/68 mmHg, HR 112 bpm, and elevated creatinine.*
- **Collaborative Problem:** *Potential Complication of Severe Acute Pancreatitis: Septic Shock / Multi-Organ Dysfunction Syndrome.*

---

**Scene 2:**

Twelve hours into David's admission, his vital signs are: BP 92/58 mmHg, HR 128 bpm, RR 24 breaths/min, temperature 39.1°C. His urine output for the past two hours is 20 mL/hr (reference ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr for a 78 kg patient = ≥39 mL/hr). He is receiving IV normal saline at 200 mL/hr as ordered. He is increasingly confused, rating pain at 9/10. Repeat labs reveal lactate 2.8 mmol/L (reference <2.0) and creatinine now 2.6 mg/dL.

*Q3: Using the SBAR format, how should the nurse communicate David's change in condition to the attending physician?*

**Answer:**
- **Situation:** "Dr. Patel, this is the nurse calling from 3 West regarding David Reyes in Room 312. He is showing signs of clinical deterioration and I am concerned about septic shock."
- **Background:** "He is a 61-year-old man admitted 12 hours ago for severe acute pancreatitis. He has a history of alcohol use disorder and hypertension. He's been on NS at 200 mL/hr, IV morphine, and IV ondansetron."
- **Assessment:** "Over the past two hours, his blood pressure has dropped to 92/58, his heart rate is 128, his temperature is 39.1°C, and his urine output is only 20 mL/hr — well below goal. Repeat labs show lactate of 2.8 and creatinine risen to 2.6 from 1.9 this morning. He is increasingly confused. These findings are consistent with septic shock criteria and worsening acute kidney injury."
- **Recommendation:** "I'm requesting that you come evaluate him immediately. I'd also like to discuss activating the sepsis protocol, increasing IV fluids, obtaining blood cultures, initiating broad-spectrum antibiotics, and considering ICU transfer."

---

**Scene 3:**

David is transferred to the ICU, where he receives aggressive fluid resuscitation, vasopressors, and broad-spectrum antibiotics. He is intubated for respiratory failure on day 2 and remains critically ill for five days before stabilizing. On day 9, he is extubated and transferred back to the medical-surgical unit. He is beginning to eat a low-fat clear liquid diet, and his creatinine has returned to near-baseline (1.3 mg/dL). He is anxious, fatigued, and asking many questions about what happened to him. His wife is present daily and is also distressed.

*Q4: Using the nursing process (ADPIE), outline a care plan for David's post-ICU medical-surgical phase, with at least two nursing diagnoses, SMART outcomes for each, and two interventions per diagnosis.*

**Answer:**
- **Nursing Diagnosis 1:** *Activity Intolerance related to prolonged ICU bed rest and critical illness as evidenced by fatigue, weakness with position changes, and inability to ambulate without assistance.*
  - **SMART Outcome:** Patient will ambulate 50 feet in the hallway with two-person assist by post-transfer day 2.
  - **Interventions:** (1) Collaborate with physical therapy to initiate progressive mobility protocol on day 1 of return to the medical-surgical unit. (2) Monitor vital signs before, during, and after each ambulation session; discontinue if HR increases >20 bpm above baseline or patient reports dyspnea.
- **Nursing Diagnosis 2:** *Anxiety related to prolonged critical illness, ICU experience, and uncertainty about recovery as evidenced by patient statements of distress, frequent questions about prognosis, and observed restlessness.*
  - **SMART Outcome:** Patient will verbalize two coping strategies and report anxiety ≤4/10 on a 0–10 scale by end of shift.
  - **Interventions:** (1) Provide a structured, patient-paced explanation of the sequence of events during his hospitalization, using clear, non-technical language; allow time for questions. (2) Refer to social work and/or clinical psychology for post-ICU psychological support; include David's wife in discussions about available resources, with patient's permission.
