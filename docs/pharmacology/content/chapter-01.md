# Chapter 1: Introduction to Nursing Pharmacology: History, Scope, and Relevance

## Learning Objectives

Upon completing this chapter, the student will be able to:

1. Define pharmacology and distinguish it from related disciplines including toxicology, pharmacotherapeutics, and pharmacognosy.
2. Describe the historical milestones that shaped the development of modern pharmacology and the nursing role in drug therapy.
3. Explain the nurse's professional, legal, and ethical responsibilities related to medication administration.
4. Apply the six rights of medication administration to clinical scenarios.
5. Identify major drug information resources used by nurses in clinical practice.
6. Discuss the impact of the nursing process on safe, effective pharmacologic care.
7. Explain how evidence-based practice guides pharmacologic decision-making.

---

## Key Terms

- **Pharmacology** — The science that studies the origin, nature, chemistry, effects, and uses of drugs.
- **Drug** — Any chemical substance that alters physiologic function in a living organism.
- **Pharmacotherapeutics** — The use of drugs to prevent, diagnose, or treat disease.
- **Toxicology** — The study of poisons and their effects, including the harmful effects of drugs at excessive doses.
- **Pharmacognosy** — The study of natural drug sources (plants, animals, minerals).
- **Pharmacokinetics** — What the body does to a drug (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion).
- **Pharmacodynamics** — What the drug does to the body (mechanism of action, effects).
- **Six Rights** — The framework for safe medication administration: right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time, and right documentation.
- **Evidence-Based Practice (EBP)** — Integration of best research evidence, clinical expertise, and patient values to guide therapeutic decisions.
- **Medication Reconciliation** — The process of comparing a patient's medication orders against all medications the patient is currently taking.

## 1.1 What Is Pharmacology?

Pharmacology is a broad biomedical science that encompasses the study of drugs—their sources, chemical composition, mechanisms of action, uses in treating disease, and potential for harm. Derived from the Greek *pharmakon* (drug or poison) and *logos* (study), pharmacology sits at the intersection of chemistry, physiology, pathology, and clinical medicine.

Nurses do not prescribe most medications independently, yet they occupy a uniquely central position in drug therapy. A nurse is almost always the last professional to check a medication order before a drug reaches the patient and the first clinician to observe and document the drug's effects. This "final check" role makes pharmacologic knowledge not merely academic but a direct patient safety imperative.

### 1.1.1 Subdivisions of Pharmacology

Several subfields define the landscape of pharmacologic science:

| Subfield | Definition | Nursing Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Pharmacokinetics** | Study of drug movement through the body (ADME) | Guides dose timing, route selection, renal/hepatic dose adjustments |
| **Pharmacodynamics** | Study of drug mechanisms and concentration–effect relationships | Explains therapeutic and adverse effects |
| **Pharmacotherapeutics** | Clinical application of drugs to treat, prevent, or diagnose disease | Core content of clinical pharmacology courses |
| **Toxicology** | Study of harmful drug effects and poisoning | Essential for overdose recognition and antidote administration |
| **Pharmacognosy** | Study of natural drug sources | Relevant to herbal supplement counseling |
| **Pharmacokinetics/pharmacodynamics (PK/PD) modeling** | Mathematical description of drug behavior | Informs individualized dosing, especially for antimicrobials |

## 1.2 A Brief History of Pharmacology

### 1.2.1 Ancient and Pre-Scientific Era

Human use of plant-based remedies dates to prehistoric times. Ancient Egyptian papyri (c. 1550 BCE), including the Ebers Papyrus, catalogued hundreds of plant preparations used as medicines. Hippocrates (c. 460–370 BCE) advocated rational, observation-based treatment and documented the use of willow bark (containing salicylate compounds) for fever and pain—a discovery that eventually led to aspirin. Dioscorides' *De Materia Medica* (c. 50–70 CE) remained a standard pharmacopeia reference for more than 1,500 years.

### 1.2.2 The Scientific Revolution and Emergence of Modern Pharmacology

The 17th through 19th centuries witnessed the systematic application of chemistry and physiology to drug discovery:

- **William Withering** (1785) documented the cardiac effects of digitalis (foxglove) in *An Account of the Foxglove*, establishing a model for systematic clinical drug observation.
- **Friedrich Sertürner** (1804) isolated morphine from opium, demonstrating that single active compounds could be extracted from plant mixtures—a foundational concept in modern drug development.
- **Claude Bernard** (1850s) used controlled animal experiments to demonstrate how curare blocks neuromuscular transmission, establishing the experimental model for pharmacodynamics.
- **Oswald Schmiedeberg** (1838–1921), often called the "father of modern pharmacology," founded the first academic pharmacology laboratory and trained the generation of scientists who established the discipline internationally.
- **Paul Ehrlich** (1908 Nobel Prize) introduced the concept of the "magic bullet"—a chemical compound selective for disease-causing organisms—and developed Salvarsan (arsphenamine) for syphilis, the first modern chemotherapeutic agent (1909).

### 1.2.3 The 20th Century: The Drug Revolution

The 20th century saw an unprecedented expansion in drug development:

- **Insulin** (Banting and Best, 1921) transformed type 1 diabetes from a fatal disease to a manageable condition.
- **Penicillin** (Fleming, 1928; Florey and Chain, 1940) inaugurated the antibiotic era, saving millions of lives in World War II and beyond.
- **Sulfonamides** became the first systemic antibacterial agents available in the 1930s.
- **Thalidomide tragedy** (late 1950s–early 1960s) — Thalidomide, used as a sedative in pregnant women, caused severe limb malformations (phocomelia) in thousands of infants, leading to fundamental reforms in drug regulation. In the United States, the **Kefauver-Harris Amendment (1962)** to the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act mandated proof of both safety **and** efficacy before FDA approval.
- **Receptor theory** matured during the mid-20th century, enabling rational drug design based on specific molecular targets.
- **Recombinant DNA technology** (1970s onward) enabled the production of biologics such as human insulin (1982), erythropoietin, and monoclonal antibodies.

### 1.2.4 Contemporary Pharmacology: Personalized and Precision Medicine

Twenty-first century pharmacology is characterized by:

- **Pharmacogenomics**: The study of how genetic variation influences drug response. Clinically relevant examples include CYP2D6 polymorphisms affecting codeine metabolism, VKORC1 and CYP2C9 variants influencing warfarin dosing sensitivity, and TPMT polymorphisms affecting thiopurine (azathioprine, mercaptopurine) toxicity risk.
- **Biologics and biosimilars**: Monoclonal antibodies, fusion proteins, and cell therapies represent a rapidly growing drug class.
- **Targeted oncology**: Kinase inhibitors, immune checkpoint inhibitors, and CAR-T therapies are redefining cancer treatment.
- **Antimicrobial stewardship**: Growing recognition of antibiotic resistance has made judicious prescribing and nursing education a global public health priority.

## 1.3 The Nurse's Role in Pharmacology

### 1.3.1 Overview of Nursing Responsibilities

Nurses bear a multifaceted responsibility in drug therapy that extends far beyond the mechanical act of administering a pill or infusion. The American Nurses Association (ANA) *Nursing: Scope and Standards of Practice* (4th ed., 2021) identifies competencies related to pharmacologic management across all practice settings. Key nursing responsibilities include:

1. **Assessment**: Obtaining a complete medication history, including prescription drugs, over-the-counter (OTC) medications, herbals, and supplements; identifying allergies and adverse reactions; and assessing relevant laboratory values and physiologic parameters before administration.
2. **Drug preparation and administration**: Safely preparing, calculating, and administering medications according to prescribed orders and established protocols.
3. **Monitoring**: Observing for therapeutic effects, adverse drug reactions (ADRs), toxicity, and drug-drug or drug-food interactions.
4. **Patient and family education**: Teaching patients the purpose, dose, schedule, storage, and adverse effects of their medications.
5. **Documentation**: Accurate, timely recording of medication administration and patient responses.
6. **Advocacy**: Questioning orders that appear incorrect or unsafe; communicating concerns to prescribers.
7. **Medication reconciliation**: Ensuring accurate medication lists at all transitions of care (admission, transfer, discharge).

### 1.3.2 Advanced Practice and Prescriptive Authority

Advanced Practice Registered Nurses (APRNs)—including Nurse Practitioners (NPs), Clinical Nurse Specialists (CNSs), Certified Registered Nurse Anesthetists (CRNAs), and Certified Nurse-Midwives (CNMs)—hold prescriptive authority in all U.S. states and territories, although the scope varies by state. APRNs must demonstrate advanced competency in pharmacology, including drug selection, dosing, monitoring, and patient education.

## 1.4 Legal and Regulatory Framework for Drug Use

### 1.4.1 Federal Drug Law in the United States

The principal federal statutes governing drug development, approval, and use include:

| Legislation | Year | Key Provision |
|---|---|---|
| Pure Food and Drug Act | 1906 | Prohibited adulteration and mislabeling of foods and drugs |
| Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA) | 1938 | Required proof of safety before marketing; triggered by sulfanilamide tragedy |
| Durham-Humphrey Amendment | 1951 | Distinguished prescription drugs from OTC drugs |
| Kefauver-Harris Amendment | 1962 | Required proof of efficacy; mandated informed consent in clinical trials |
| Controlled Substances Act (CSA) | 1970 | Established DEA schedules I–V based on abuse potential |
| Orphan Drug Act | 1983 | Incentivized development of drugs for rare diseases |
| Prescription Drug User Fee Act (PDUFA) | 1992 | Allowed FDA to collect fees from manufacturers to accelerate review |
| Biologics Price Competition and Innovation Act (BPCIA) | 2010 | Created pathway for biosimilar approval |

### 1.4.2 DEA Controlled Substance Schedules

The Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) classifies controlled substances into five schedules based on accepted medical use and potential for abuse or dependence:

| Schedule | Characteristics | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **I** | High abuse potential; no accepted medical use in the U.S. | Heroin, LSD, marijuana (federal), ecstasy |
| **II** | High abuse potential; accepted medical use; may lead to severe dependence | Morphine, oxycodone, fentanyl, amphetamine, methylphenidate |
| **III** | Moderate abuse potential; accepted medical use | Buprenorphine, anabolic steroids, ketamine |
| **IV** | Lower abuse potential relative to Schedule III | Benzodiazepines, zolpidem, carisoprodol |
| **V** | Lowest abuse potential of controlled substances | Cough preparations containing codeine, pregabalin |

Nurses who administer controlled substances must follow strict documentation, counting, and waste procedures in accordance with institutional policy and state law.

### 1.4.3 Canadian Drug Regulation

In Canada, Health Canada regulates drugs under the *Food and Drugs Act*. Controlled drugs and substances are governed by the *Controlled Drugs and Substances Act (CDSA)*. Canadian nurses follow provincial regulatory body standards (e.g., College of Nurses of Ontario) in addition to federal law.

## 1.5 Drug Information Resources

Nurses rely on a variety of evidence-based references to guide medication administration:

| Resource | Format | Strengths |
|---|---|---|
| **Prescribers' Digital Reference (PDR)** | Print/online | Comprehensive prescribing information |
| **Clinical Pharmacology and Biologic** | Online database | Drug interactions, IV compatibility |
| **Lexicomp / UpToDate** | Online | Point-of-care access; frequently updated |
| **Micromedex** | Online | Toxicology, monographs, drug interactions |
| **Epocrates** | Mobile app | Fast bedside reference |
| **American Hospital Formulary Service (AHFS) Drug Information** | Print/online | Official U.S. formulary standard |
| **Nursing Drug Handbook (Lippincott)** | Print/mobile | Nursing-focused, patient teaching sections |
| **Package Insert (Prescribing Information)** | FDA-approved document | Legal standard; includes all FDA-approved indications and warnings |

> **Clinical Pearl:** When in doubt, consult the package insert or a current point-of-care database before administering any unfamiliar drug. Never rely solely on memory for high-alert medications.

## 1.6 The Six Rights of Medication Administration

Safe medication administration requires adherence to the **six rights** as a minimum safety standard. Some institutions expand this framework to nine or ten rights.

### Six Rights

1. **Right Patient**: Verify the patient's identity using at least two patient identifiers (e.g., name and date of birth or medical record number). Never rely on room number alone.
2. **Right Drug**: Confirm the drug name (generic and brand), concentration, and form match the order. Be alert to look-alike/sound-alike (LASA) drug names.
3. **Right Dose**: Calculate the dose independently. Double-check high-alert medication calculations with a second nurse per institutional policy.
4. **Right Route**: Confirm the prescribed route is appropriate for the patient's condition and the drug's formulation (e.g., oral, IV, IM, subcutaneous, topical).
5. **Right Time**: Administer medications at the scheduled time within institutional policy windows (typically ±30 minutes for most drugs, ±60 minutes for time-critical medications).
6. **Right Documentation**: Record the administration immediately after giving the drug, including dose, route, site (for injectables), and the patient's response.

### Additional Rights Recognized by Many Institutions

- **Right Reason**: Confirm the drug is appropriate for the patient's clinical condition.
- **Right Response**: Assess and document expected therapeutic outcomes.
- **Right to Refuse**: Respect the patient's informed right to decline medication.
- **Right Education**: Provide accurate, patient-centered teaching about the drug.

## 1.7 The Nursing Process Applied to Pharmacology

The nursing process—Assessment, Diagnosis, Planning, Implementation, and Evaluation (ADPIE)—provides a systematic framework for pharmacologic care.

### Assessment

- Obtain a comprehensive medication history including prescription, OTC, herbal, and recreational substances.
- Identify drug allergies and previous adverse reactions (document reaction type, not just "allergy").
- Review relevant laboratory values (renal function, hepatic function, CBC, serum drug levels).
- Assess vital signs, weight (for weight-based dosing), and clinical status.
- Determine the patient's health literacy, language preferences, and ability to self-administer.

### Nursing Diagnosis (NANDA-I)

Common nursing diagnoses related to pharmacotherapy include:
- *Deficient Knowledge* related to new medication regimen
- *Risk for Injury* related to adverse drug effects (e.g., fall risk from sedation, bleeding risk from anticoagulants)
- *Noncompliance* related to cost, side effects, or misunderstanding
- *Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements* related to medication-induced nausea

### Planning

- Establish measurable goals (e.g., "Patient will verbalize the purpose of metformin and identify hypoglycemia symptoms before discharge").
- Identify potential drug-drug, drug-food, and drug-disease interactions.
- Plan monitoring parameters (e.g., blood pressure before each antihypertensive dose).

### Implementation

- Adhere to the six rights.
- Use aseptic technique for parenteral medications.
- Administer high-alert medications according to institutional double-check policies.
- Provide timely patient education.

### Evaluation

- Compare actual patient response to expected therapeutic outcomes.
- Document and report adverse drug reactions (ADRs) through institutional incident-reporting systems and, when appropriate, to the FDA MedWatch program.
- Reassess the continuing need for each medication (deprescribing when appropriate).

## 1.8 Evidence-Based Practice and Pharmacology

Evidence-based practice (EBP) integrates three components:
1. **Best available evidence** — randomized controlled trials (RCTs), systematic reviews, and clinical practice guidelines.
2. **Clinical expertise** — the nurse's own knowledge and judgment.
3. **Patient values and preferences** — individual goals, cultural beliefs, and financial considerations.

In pharmacology, EBP is applied by:
- Following formulary guidelines and clinical pathways derived from national guidelines (e.g., JNC 8 for hypertension, ADA Standards of Care for diabetes, IDSA guidelines for infections).
- Critically appraising medication studies for bias, statistical significance, and clinical significance.
- Recognizing that statistical significance (p < 0.05) does not always translate to clinically meaningful benefit.
- Applying the **number needed to treat (NNT)** and **number needed to harm (NNH)** to contextualize drug benefits and risks for patients.

## 1.9 Patient Safety and High-Alert Medications

The Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) publishes a list of **high-alert medications**—drugs that bear a heightened risk of causing significant patient harm when used in error. Common categories include:

- Concentrated electrolytes (e.g., KCl for injection)
- Anticoagulants (warfarin, heparin, direct oral anticoagulants)
- Opioids
- Insulin
- Hypertonic saline
- Chemotherapy agents

> **Safety Alert:** The ISMP recommends that concentrated potassium chloride (KCl) for injection never be stored on general patient care units, given the risk of fatal cardiac dysrhythmia if administered undiluted or at high rates. Always follow institutional protocol.

### Strategies to Reduce Medication Errors

- **Tall-man lettering**: HYDROmorphone vs. HYDROXYzine; DOPamine vs. DOBUTamine.
- **Barcode medication administration (BCMA)**: Scanning both the patient's wristband and the medication before administration.
- **Smart IV pumps with dose-error reduction software (DERS)**: Drug libraries that alert clinicians to doses outside safe limits.
- **Independent double-checks**: Two-nurse verification for high-alert drugs.
- **Pharmacy review**: Pharmacist review of all new orders before first-dose administration.

## 1.10 Cultural Considerations in Pharmacology

Cultural background, race, ethnicity, and socioeconomic status influence pharmacologic response and adherence:

- **Pharmacogenomic variation**: Certain CYP450 enzyme polymorphisms (e.g., CYP2C19 poor metabolizers) are more prevalent in specific ethnic groups, affecting drug metabolism.
- **Herbal and traditional medicine use**: Patients from many cultures use traditional remedies alongside prescribed drugs; nurses must ask about these without judgment, as significant interactions exist (e.g., St. John's wort reduces efficacy of many drugs through CYP3A4 induction).
- **Health literacy**: Approximately 36% of U.S. adults have limited health literacy, making clear, simple medication instruction essential.
- **Cost and access**: High out-of-pocket costs are a leading cause of non-adherence; nurses should refer patients to patient assistance programs and generic alternatives.

## Summary

Pharmacology is the scientific foundation upon which safe, effective nursing medication practice is built. The history of pharmacology reflects a trajectory from empirical plant-based remedies to evidence-based, molecularly targeted therapies. Nurses play an irreplaceable role in the medication use process—from assessment and administration to monitoring, education, and advocacy. The six rights of medication administration, the nursing process, evidence-based practice, and knowledge of legal and regulatory frameworks together constitute the cornerstone of pharmacologic nursing competence. Mastery of these principles is prerequisite to all subsequent chapters in this textbook.

## NCLEX-Style Review Questions

**1.** A nurse is preparing to administer a scheduled dose of metoprolol succinate to a patient. Before administration, the nurse assesses the patient's heart rate at 48 beats/min. Which action is most appropriate?

- A. Administer the medication as scheduled.
- B. Hold the medication and notify the prescriber.
- C. Administer half the prescribed dose.
- D. Ask the patient whether they feel symptomatic before deciding.

**Correct answer: B.** Metoprolol is a beta-blocker; bradycardia (heart rate < 60 beats/min) is a common parameter for withholding the dose and notifying the prescriber.

---

**2.** A patient asks the nurse, "Why do I have to take this new blood thinner?" The nurse recognizes that this question is best addressed under which right of medication administration?

- A. Right time
- B. Right dose
- C. Right reason
- D. Right documentation

**Correct answer: C.** The right reason requires nurses to confirm the rationale for each drug and to provide patient education that explains why the medication is needed.

---

**3.** Which federal legislation first required manufacturers to demonstrate both safety **and** efficacy before a drug could be marketed in the United States?

- A. Pure Food and Drug Act (1906)
- B. Durham-Humphrey Amendment (1951)
- C. Kefauver-Harris Amendment (1962)
- D. Controlled Substances Act (1970)

**Correct answer: C.** The Kefauver-Harris Amendment (1962) was enacted after the thalidomide tragedy and required proof of efficacy in addition to safety.

---

**4.** A nurse discovers that a patient has been taking St. John's wort daily for depression. Which pharmacologic concern is most important to document and report to the prescriber?

- A. St. John's wort is a Schedule IV controlled substance.
- B. St. John's wort is a potent CYP3A4 inducer that may reduce the efficacy of many prescribed drugs.
- C. St. John's wort can cause a potentially fatal serotonin syndrome if taken with any herb.
- D. St. John's wort has no known drug interactions.

**Correct answer: B.** St. John's wort induces CYP3A4 and other CYP enzymes, increasing the metabolism and reducing blood levels of many drugs including antiretrovirals, oral contraceptives, cyclosporine, and warfarin.

---

**5.** When reviewing a patient's medication administration record, the nurse notes that morphine 4 mg IV was documented as administered at 0900 but the patient states no one gave them anything for pain until 1000. This documentation discrepancy represents a failure of which right?

- A. Right patient
- B. Right dose
- C. Right route
- D. Right documentation

**Correct answer: D.** Accurate, immediate documentation after administration is the right of documentation. Pre-charting (documenting before actually giving the drug) is unsafe and a policy violation.

## Case Study: Medication Safety in a Post-Surgical Patient

**Scenario:** Mr. T.W. is a 68-year-old male admitted after a left total knee replacement. His past medical history includes atrial fibrillation (on warfarin at home), stage 3 chronic kidney disease (CKD), and hypertension. Postoperative orders include hydromorphone 0.5 mg IV PRN q3h for pain, lisinopril 10 mg PO daily, and warfarin per pharmacy dosing protocol. The nurse receives a verbal order from a resident to add ketorolac 15 mg IV q6h for pain management.

**Discussion Questions:**

1. What concerns does the combination of warfarin and ketorolac raise, and how should the nurse respond to the verbal order?
2. How does CKD affect the selection and dosing of analgesics in this patient?
3. Apply the six rights to the administration of Mr. T.W.'s hydromorphone order. What assessment data must the nurse obtain before each dose?
4. What patient education should the nurse provide regarding warfarin before Mr. T.W.'s discharge?

**Key Points:**
- Ketorolac (an NSAID) inhibits platelet aggregation and can cause gastrointestinal bleeding; combined with warfarin, it significantly increases hemorrhagic risk and should be avoided or used with extreme caution.
- CKD reduces renal clearance of many analgesics and their active metabolites (e.g., hydromorphone-3-glucuronide may accumulate and cause neuroexcitation).
- Pre-dose hydromorphone assessment must include respiratory rate, sedation level, blood pressure, pain score, and current oxygen saturation.
- Warfarin education should cover dietary vitamin K consistency, the need for regular INR monitoring, bleeding precautions, and drug/supplement interactions.
